# The Man Who Sold His Shadow

The man who sold his shadow came back to town on a Tuesday, in the flat light of noon, when nobody casts much of one anyway.

He took a room above the chandler's and paid in coins that were cold to the touch. He was polite; he tipped; he remembered the baker's name, and the baker, who had never told him it, remembered that. Money, in that town, was a quiet thing, and his was quieter than most.

There was the matter of the window shades, drawn against the noon. There was the way the lamplighter crossed the street rather than pass him. There was his habit of keeping to the shaded side, even in January. There was, above all, the fact that nobody could name what was wrong — only that something had arrived. By nightfall the dogs had begun to growl at doorways, at gateposts, at the empty space beside his boots.

It was the children, who notice everything, who understood first: not only the missing shadow, not only the cold coins, not only the way he flinched from lamplight like a man remembering a debt. They stopped their hopscotch as he passed, and the chalk lines lay on the pavement ==like the outline of a man==.

He knew they knew. That is the trouble with a bargain — half of it *always* shows.
