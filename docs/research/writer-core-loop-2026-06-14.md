## Synthesis

# Strop Synthesis — The Diagnosis-First Editor and the Real Writer's Loop

*Synthesis across seven dossiers: core-loop, versioning, parallel-edits, editor-relationship, latency-and-AI, editorial-agreement, tooling-gaps. The job here is not to summarize seven reports but to find the load-bearing structure that runs through all of them — and to convert it into things Strop can build.*

---

## 0. The one idea that ties all seven dossiers together

Every dossier, approached from a different angle, lands on the same shape: **writing alternates between a GENERATE mode and an EVALUATE mode, and almost every tool failure is a failure to respect which mode the writer is in.** Grammarly fires evaluation into the generate window. Word's track-changes dumps the whole evaluation flood at once with no altitude. Version history answers an evaluation question (*which version was alive?*) with generation-era metadata (timestamps). AI generators collapse the two modes into one anchored slurry that averages voice toward the mean.

Strop's entire thesis — diagnosis-only, never-rewrite, anti-homogenization, local-first — is best understood as **mode discipline made into a product**: be silent and safe during GENERATE, be a named-problem-and-a-question editor during EVALUATE, and never supply the replacement text that would let the writer's voice drift toward a statistical center. Hold that frame and the seven dossiers stop being seven topics and become one machine.

## 1. The Consolidated Core-Loop Model

A real writing day is **not a pipeline**. Flower & Hayes' five years of think-aloud protocols found planning, drafting, and reviewing are "non-linear and highly embedded" — any process nests inside any other ([Flower & Hayes Cognitive Process Theory](https://www.yomu.ai/blog/flower-and-hayes-cognitive-process-theory-of-writing)). So Strop must model **modes the writer re-enters many times**, not stages it completes once.

### The two macro-modes and three sub-modes

```
                 ┌──────────────  GENERATE  ──────────────┐   ┌────────── EVALUATE ──────────┐
   (cold start)  │   PLANNING            DRAFTING          │   │   REVIEWING (3 altitudes)    │
       ●────────▶│  beats / cards   prose bursts (P-bursts)│──▶│  developmental → line → copy │
                 │  diagnosis OFF    diagnosis SILENT       │   │  diagnosis SURFACES, paced   │
                 └─────────────────────────────────────────┘   └──────────────────────────────┘
                              ▲                                          │
                              └──────────────  (re-enters drafting)  ◀───┘
```

- **PLANNING** (generate, pre-prose): index cards / beat sheets — "see the movie before you start" ([Save the Cat Beat Sheet](https://reedsy.com/blog/guide/story-structure/save-the-cat-beat-sheet/)). Diagnosis has nothing to say yet.
- **DRAFTING** (generate, prose): King's "door closed" draft, written for you alone ([Stephen King's Writing Process](https://www.shortform.com/blog/stephen-kings-writing-process/)). Text comes in **bursts punctuated by pauses**; fluency = longer bursts, fewer revision episodes ([Chenoweth & Hayes, Fluency in Writing](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0741088301018001004)). **Evaluation here is the cardinal sin** — Elbow: premature editing "makes writing dead… your voice is damped out by all the interruptions" ([Elbow, Freewriting](https://www.research.ucsb.edu/sites/default/files/RD/docs/FREEWRITING-by-Peter-Elbow.pdf)).
- **REVIEWING** (evaluate): real revision is meaning-level "re-seeing," not word-swapping; novices do "thesaurus-philosophy" lexical tinkering ([Sommers, Revision Strategies](http://muwriting.wdfiles.com/local--files/materials-for-class/revision.strategies.pdf)). It has a **mandatory altitude order**: developmental → line → copy, "to prevent paying to copyedit passages that will later be cut" ([Reedsy — Types of Editing](https://blog.reedsy.com/guide/editing/)).

### The switches (where Strop earns or loses the writer)

The modes matter less than the **transitions between them** — that is where every tool fails and where Strop intervenes:

1. **IGNITION** — nothing → drafting. The hardest moment of the day. Pressfield's "Resistance"; acute task-initiation deficit for the ADHD-minded writer ([War of Art](https://www.shortform.com/blog/steven-pressfield-resistance/); [Writers With ADHD](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/writers-with-adhd/)). → *Strop's "zero blocking questions at open."*
2. **THE CARDINAL GUARD** — drafting ⇄ evaluating *mid-burst*. The dangerous, involuntary crossing. Block is caused by "inflexible rules" and premature self-monitoring ([Mike Rose, Rigid Rules](https://gennavievegray.wordpress.com/2013/09/20/mike-roses-rigid-rules-inflexible-plans-and-the-stifling-of-language-a-cognitivist-analysis-of-writers-block/)). → *Strop must prevent the writer (and itself) from crossing during a burst.*
3. **HANDOFF (stopping)** — Hemingway: "always stop when you are going good and when you know what will happen next… you will never be stuck" ([Hemingway's Routine](https://fs.blog/hemingways-routine/)).
4. **RE-ENTRY (next day)** — the warm-up re-read is the single most common ritual *and* the most common trap: re-immersion slides into line-editing and the session produces zero new words ([Warm-Up Routine](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/warming-up/)).
5. **ALTITUDE DESCENT** — inside review, dropping from developmental to line to copy, never the reverse.
6. **BACKTRACK** — review → recover an earlier state (covered in 4a).
7. **FORK** — "try it both ways" → reconcile by rewriting (covered in 4b).

**Design consequence:** Strop already records the keystroke stream it needs to detect mode behaviorally — rapid P-bursts = drafting (stay silent); sustained pauses + scroll-back + cursor jumps = reviewing (surface cards) ([Chenoweth & Hayes](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0741088301018001004)). The unfilled niche across the whole tool landscape is exactly this: every tool makes the writer switch modes **manually**, and **none gates feedback by mode** ([Scrivener Composition Mode](https://www.literatureandlatte.com/blog/distraction-free-writing-with-scrivener)).

## 2. Cross-Cutting Pain Points, Ranked by How Badly They Bite

These are the frustrations that recur across **three or more** dossiers. Ranked by breadth × severity.

**P1 — Evaluation fired into the generate window (kills momentum *and* voice).** The single most-cited craft rule (separate drafting from editing) and the single most-cited tool failure. Real-time suggestions are "intrusive… messing up thought process" ([Copilot Suggestions study](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2504.06808)); craft writers literally advise "turn off your spell checkers when drafting" ([Rachel Rowlands](https://racheljrowlands.com/2022/05/30/how-to-silence-your-inner-editor-when-drafting/)). Bites worst because it costs *both* flow and the author's voice ([Elbow](https://www.research.ucsb.edu/sites/default/files/RD/docs/FREEWRITING-by-Peter-Elbow.pdf)).

**P2 — The undifferentiated flood / wall.** Same shape in five dossiers: the "dizzying" red track-changes flood that weights a comma identically to "this chapter has no stakes" ([Spoonbridge](https://spoonbridgepress.com/track-changes-in-word/)); ProWritingAid's 25-report analysis-paralysis ([latency dossier]); the "wall of timestamps"; the ADHD shame-wall of simultaneous corrections. The flood *triggers the very avoidance* that stops writing.

**P3 — Voice homogenization toward the mean.** The empirical core of the thesis. AI assistance raises individual quality but makes the corpus 10.7% more similar ([Doshi & Hauser, Science Advances](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.adn5290)); flattens non-Western voice (classifier accuracy 90.6%→83.5%) ([Cornell Chronicle](https://news.cornell.edu/stories/2025/04/ai-suggestions-make-writing-more-generic-western)). Mechanism = **anchoring on supplied text**. Existential, not cosmetic.

**P4 — History navigable only by mechanism, never by meaning.** Writers backtrack asking "where was this still alive?" and get timestamps and red/blue diffs ([MTSU — What's the Diff?](https://mtsu.pressbooks.pub/engl1020/chapter/version-history/)). The thing they want back is mood/energy/structure, "the version before I wrung the life out of it" ([Calonne](https://wacclearinghouse.org/books/horning_revision/chapter9.pdf)).

**P5 — The foresight paradox and fear of loss.** You must snapshot *before* the edit goes wrong, but the need is felt *after* ([Scrivener Snapshots](https://www.literatureandlatte.com/blog/use-snapshots-in-scrivener-to-save-versions-of-your-projects)). The inherited Hemingway-suitcase terror ([Farnam Street](https://fs.blog/hemingway-suitcase/)) makes deleting feel final, so writers hoard `final.FINAL.v2(real).docx` ([xkcd 1459](https://xkcd.com/1459/)). It makes writers cling and over-edit instead of cutting boldly.

**P6 — No place to hold two takes; merging kills the prose.** No prose tool holds Version A and B in place; mechanical merge is "washing garbage… I've washed all the life out of the scene" ([Crusie, Rewrite Exhaustion](https://www.goodreads.com/author_blog_posts/14916369-rewrite-exhaustion)).

**P7 — Tinkering-forever / never-finishing.** Instant, unlimited feedback removes the natural stopping points the human editor's latency provided ([Jane Friedman — Before You Hire a Developmental Editor](https://janefriedman.com/before-you-hire-a-developmental-editor-what-you-need-to-know/)); feature-maximal tools fuel perfectionism.

**P8 — Setup tax / condescension-and-sycophancy.** Two tone-and-friction failures bundled: blank-slate tools lose writers to "three weeks building a vault, never wrote a scene" ([Obsidian for Fiction Writers](https://loreteller.com/learn/obsidian-fiction-writers-guide/)); and feedback that either scolds ("condescending high-school English teacher") or flatters ("AI just tells them their work is wonderful") destroys trust.

## 3. The Big Opportunities for Strop

Each is mapped to a stated Strop principle, with the writer need, a concrete UX/metaphor, and how it holds the line on diagnosis-only + anti-homogenization + local-first.

### O1 — The Silent Draft (mode-gated diagnosis)
- **Principle:** "Enough support, not neutrality" + zero blocking questions at open.
- **Need:** Protect the burst/flow window (P1); the cardinal-guard switch.
- **UX/metaphor:** A **"door"** — closed while drafting, open in review (King's metaphor made literal). Cards are computed silently and *queued*; nothing renders in the margin until the writer enters Review (manual toggle v1; behavioral P-burst inference later, always defaulting ambiguous states to **quiet**).
- **Stays true:** No streaming = no anchoring = no homogenization ([Doshi & Hauser](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.adn5290)); the keystroke signal is already local; it never rewrites, it just *waits*.

### O2 — The Altitude Ladder + the Editorial Letter
- **Principle:** Diagnosis-first + opinionated defaults.
- **Need:** A big-picture read and the right *order* (P2, P4); avoid the novice thesaurus-trap ([Sommers](http://muwriting.wdfiles.com/local--files/materials-for-class/revision.strategies.pdf)).
- **UX/metaphor:** Every card declares a level (structural / line / copy); **copy-level cards are suppressed while any structural card is open** ([Reedsy — Types of Editing](https://blog.reedsy.com/guide/editing/)). Add a distinct, manuscript-level **editorial-letter** surface — a *small* number of whole-book diagnoses delivered **strengths-first**, as "a vote of confidence" and "a roadmap for revision" ([Fawkes — How I Write an Editorial Letter](https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2019/03/how-i-write-editorial-letter-or.html)).
- **Stays true:** The letter names *effect*, never prose; strengths-first prevents the defensiveness spiral that is especially toxic for the ADHD-minded writer.

### O3 — History You Navigate by Meaning and Voice
- **Principle:** Restart-surviving keystroke history + named checkpoints + anti-homogenization.
- **Need:** Recover meaning/energy, not timestamps (P4).
- **UX/metaphor:** A **voice-trajectory sparkline** (stylometric distance from the writer's *own* baseline, not a corpus) with checkpoints as labeled stations. Crucially, **a checkpoint is auto-labeled by the diagnosis cards that were open at that moment** — "the version before I over-controlled the dialogue" — deriving the semantic *why* nobody else stores, without demanding the author name versions (settings are apologies).
- **Stays true:** Names a trajectory, never picks a winner; scores against the author's own voice, never a published mean (the quiet homogenization vector in AutoCrit/Fictionary); all local.

### O4 — "Try It Both Ways" + the Cuttings Drawer
- **Principle:** The fix is always the author's + anti-homogenization.
- **Need:** Hold A and B and weave by ear; cut without fear (P5, P6).
- **UX/metaphor:** Select a span → spawn **Version A / Version B anchored at the same spot**, toggled inline — Final Draft's "Alt Dialogue," finally brought to prose ([Final Draft — Alternate Dialogue](https://www.finaldraft.com/blog/give-your-characters-options-the-power-of-alternate-dialogue)). Rendered as two **whole readable passages** for the read-aloud test, never red/green confetti ([Reading Aloud — Duke](https://twp.duke.edu/sites/twp.duke.edu/files/file-attachments/reading-aloud-1.original.pdf)). The loser and all cut darlings fall into a searchable **Cuttings drawer** back-linked to origin (the sanctioned "piggy bank"/"morgue" — [Reproof](https://www.reproof.app/blog/writing-and-the-cutting-room-floor)).
- **Stays true:** **No auto-merge button, ever** — Crusie's "washing garbage" is the proof a machine stitch homogenizes prose ([Crusie](https://www.goodreads.com/author_blog_posts/14916369-rewrite-exhaustion)). The morgue is **preservation only**; Strop must never auto-suggest reusing an exiled darling (that would be voice-recycling).

### O5 — The Editorial Agreement (Continuity Canon + Voice Charter)
- **Principle:** Anti-homogenization + the fix is the author's + settings are apologies.
- **Need:** Be told what to leave alone; catch continuity drift (P3, P8).
- **UX/metaphor:** **Two halves doing opposite things.** A **Continuity Canon** (names, traits, timeline, hyphenation) *enables* drift cards ("Ch.1 her eyes are green; Ch.8 brown"). A **Voice Charter** in the author's own words *suppresses* cards ("comma splices are my rhythm — never flag them") ([Ink Editorial — Novel Style Sheet](https://www.inkeditorial.co.uk/blog/how-to-build-a-novel-style-sheet-free-template); [SFWA — Understanding the Copyeditor](https://www.sfwa.org/2009/06/23/a-writers-guide-to-understanding-the-copyeditor/)). **Born empty; inference proposes, the author ratifies.**
- **Stays true:** Strop may only ever learn *what to stop flagging*, surfaced in the open and ratified — never silent acceptance-driven adaptation, which is the exact mechanism that erodes voice ([Cornell/CHI 2025](https://news.cornell.edu/stories/2025/04/ai-suggestions-make-writing-more-generic-western)).

### O6 — The Re-Entry On-Ramp
- **Principle:** "Enough support, not neutrality" + zero blocking questions at open.
- **Need:** Defeat cold-start Resistance and the re-read-becomes-editing trap (P1, IGNITION + RE-ENTRY).
- **UX/metaphor:** A persistent **"where you stopped / what happens next"** marker (Hemingway's trick) plus a **read-only re-immersion view** that re-grounds without inviting edits ([Hemingway's Routine](https://fs.blog/hemingways-routine/)). Diagnosis stays silent until the first new keystrokes.
- **Stays true:** No interruption, no rewrite; pure ADHD task-initiation scaffolding.

### O7 — Scarcity as a Feature (the anti-flood / anti-tinker engine)
- **Principle:** "Settings are apologies" / opinionated defaults.
- **Need:** A completable backlog; no perfectionism loop (P2, P7).
- **UX/metaphor:** A **small card budget per view**; the margin gets *quieter* as you work — the opposite of a dashboard. "A dismissed card never returns" is both UX courtesy and a **cognitive forcing function** that guarantees finitude ([Buçinca et al., CHI 2021](https://arxiv.org/abs/2102.09692)). Re-diagnosis costs a deliberate author action — *never* auto-run on every keystroke just because local Ollama makes it ~free.
- **Stays true:** Restraint *is* the anti-homogenization stance applied to volume; "look it up first / don't query what you can verify" ([ACES — Writing effective author queries](https://aceseditors.org/news/2019/writing-effective-author-queries)).

## 4. Deep Dives

### 4a. Version history & backtracking — recovering MEANING, not timestamps

The failure to beat is universal: every competitor answers "what changed and when," but writers backtrack asking "where was this still good and why did I change it" ([MTSU](https://mtsu.pressbooks.pub/engl1020/chapter/version-history/)). Concrete proposals:

1. **The history panel is a trajectory, not a list.** Primary surface = a voice/energy sparkline (stylometric self-similarity against the writer's own baseline), with checkpoints as stations. Scrubbing the line answers "where was the voice strongest / before the flattening" — a read no timestamp list can give.
2. **Checkpoints carry their open diagnosis cards.** A version is labeled by the *problems it had or answered*, deriving the semantic layer everyone omits — and by **derivation**, not by asking the author to name versions (the discipline they never have — [Emma Darwin's "eight files all called Final"](https://emmadarwin.substack.com/p/itchy-bitesized-32-taming-your-draft)).
3. **Backtrack is non-local.** Don't just offer text-restore (it serves only the easy single-sentence case). Offer a diagnosis-style observation — "these three checkpoints scored highest on your own voice" — then let the author read and decide.
4. **Defuse the comparison bias.** Existing text has unearned "authority and vividness" that dominates the older draft ([Emma Darwin](https://emmadarwin.substack.com/p/itchy-bitesized-32-taming-your-draft)). Never present current-as-winner; frame as a question: "the earlier version had more momentum here; the current is more controlled — which serves the scene?"
5. **Revert is a forward edit, not a destructive commit.** Because history is continuous, restoring is just a new state you can leave again (Figma's paired-checkpoint safety, free). Say so loudly to kill the `final.FINAL.v2` reflex. **No branch DAG / undo-tree** (disorienting even to programmers); linearize restore-as-edit.
6. **For the ADHD writer, make forward motion legible.** Some revert impulses are discouragement, not a text request ("the second draft feels like a step back" — [Writers in the Storm](https://writersinthestormblog.com/2025/06/when-the-second-draft-feels-like-a-step-back/)). A "parade of small rewards" progress view meets the thrash-backward urge with evidence of motion rather than a frictionless spiral machine.

**Anti-features:** no wall of timestamps, no per-author colors, no diff confetti as the *primary* surface (keep readable inline redline only for orientation).

### 4b. Competing drafts & reconciliation — "try two, then weave" for a prose thinker

The behavior is real and deliberate (Rusch ran five concurrent versions; Crusie kept seven of one breakfast scene), and HCI backs it: parallel creation beats serial because critique lands on options, not on "your only baby" ([Dow et al., 2010](https://hci.stanford.edu/publications/2010/parallel-prototyping/ParallelPrototyping2010-final.pdf)). Concrete flow:

1. **In-place fork, in the writer's words.** "Try it both ways" — never "branch," "diff," or "merge." Both versions are co-equal and both live in keystroke history.
2. **Whole passages, for the ear.** Toggle or stack two readable takes; reserve any character-level diff strictly for the history panel.
3. **Reconciliation is an authorial rewrite, full stop.** Collapsing a fork **picks one** or **replaces both with a freshly typed third**; the loser goes to the Cuttings drawer. There is **no "combine" affordance** — "it's not a set of Lego blocks, it's a story… I don't assemble stories, I write them" ([Crusie](https://www.goodreads.com/author_blog_posts/14916369-rewrite-exhaustion)).
4. **Diagnosis operates *on* a fork without choosing.** "Version A opens on the image; B on the line of dialogue — which entry do you want the reader to land on?" Critique across options; author decides; voice never averaged.
5. **Design against rewrite-exhaustion.** When a passage accumulates several forks, surface a gentle "start this one fresh?" that keeps the surrounding context and the named diagnoses while clearing the pile to history — Crusie's "close the folder and write it fresh" as a *supported* action, not a failure.

### 4c. The editor relationship at zero latency — pacing, rounds, accept/reject memory

The human relationship is slow, asynchronous, batched, and that latency is **partly a feature**: the days between letter and revision create reflective distance that prevents defensiveness ([Jane Friedman](https://janefriedman.com/before-you-hire-a-developmental-editor-what-you-need-to-know/)). Strop inverts the latency but must not recreate the flood. Concrete:

1. **Batch into discrete PASSES, not a live keystroke stream.** A finite, completable set of cards the author works through and dismisses — simulate the editorial *rhythm* even though the compute is instant. Don't stream just because local makes it cheap; that is precisely the slot-machine trap.
2. **The card is the in-margin query, formalized — as hard constraints:** anchored to a specific span; exactly one problem; a concern + a question, never a command; reader-framed ("a reader might lose the thread here"); open-ended, not yes/no; **≤ one or two sentences "to ensure they're actually read"** ([West Coast Editorial — The art of the query](https://westcoasteditors.com/the-art-of-the-query/)).
3. **Canonical grammar: "this passage is doing X — is that intentional?"** Straight from the copyediting canon; it presupposes author competence rather than assuming error ([Copyeditor's Handbook](https://content.ucpress.edu/title/9780520286726/9780520286726_chap1.pdf)).
4. **Non-authoritative voice.** Cards read as one editor's reading ("this might be flat; does it land for you?"), never a verdict — because high confidence in AI predicts *less* author critical thinking ([Microsoft/CMU CHI 2025](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/publication/the-impact-of-generative-ai-on-critical-thinking-self-reported-reductions-in-cognitive-effort-and-confidence-effects-from-a-survey-of-knowledge-workers/)).
5. **Accept/reject MEMORY is the killer feature Word can't keep.** Strop's history + "dismissed card never returns" *is the author's own style sheet* — the durable, queryable decision log ([SFWA on the style sheet](https://www.sfwa.org/2009/06/23/a-writers-guide-to-understanding-the-copyeditor/)). Make the history panel teachable as exactly that: "what was flagged, and what you decided."
6. **Anti-tinker nudge.** "You've reworked this paragraph many times — checkpoint and move on?" converts anxious looping into a decision, replacing the editor's "rounds" with author-owned milestones.
7. **Tone red lines:** no rule-citation lectures, no "it looks funny," no sycophantic praise. Play lives in mechanics, never in condescending aesthetics ([Grammarly's pulled "Expert Review"](https://www.engadget.com/ai/grammarly-has-disabled-its-tool-offering-generative-ai-feedback-credited-to-real-writers-201614257.html) is the cautionary tale).

### 4d. The Editorial Agreement — explicit vs inferred, what it stores, failure modes

The trade's answer to "explicit vs inferred" is decisively **both, with inference always surfaced** — the copyeditor reconstructs the author's style "like a detective… and submits the lists with the finished job" ([SFWA](https://www.sfwa.org/2009/06/23/a-writers-guide-to-understanding-the-copyeditor/)).

- **What it stores — two opposite engines from one artifact:** a **Continuity Canon** (facts; *enables* drift cards) and a **Voice Charter** (leave-alone preferences in the author's words; *suppresses* cards) ([Ink Editorial](https://www.inkeditorial.co.uk/blog/how-to-build-a-novel-style-sheet-free-template)). The Charter is the headline — intentional "grammar goofs" are deliberate voice, not error ([Carol Saller](https://www.subversivecopyeditor.com/blog/2021/05/how-grammar-goofs-work-in-creative-writing.html)).
- **Explicit vs inferred:** born empty (zero blocking questions); grows by **infer-and-ratify**. Keep a hard line between **per-instance dismiss (stet)** and a **standing rule**. Never silently promote one to the other — after N identical dismissals, surface **one** card: "You've waved off comma-splice flags four times — add to your Voice Charter so I stop?"
- **Failure modes & countermeasures (borrowed wholesale from linter culture):**
  - *The blanket disable* — "leave my whole manuscript alone" / dots under every line. **Forbid it**; every rule must name a *specific* pattern ([no-abusive-eslint-disable](https://oxc.rs/docs/guide/usage/linter/rules/unicorn/no-abusive-eslint-disable)).
  - *The comfort bubble* — an agreement that silences all useful criticism; personalization to "revealed preferences" homogenizes and confirms bias ([Personalisation within bounds](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2303.05453)). Counter: periodically resurface stale/over-broad rules — "You told me never to flag fragments 90 days ago — still right?" (the equivalent of `report-unused-disable-directives`).
  - *The silent-learning vector* — the **red line**: never learn from accept/reject to reshape *what* it suggests; only ever learn what to *stop* flagging, and even that confirmed in the open.
- **Persistence:** host in version history; version the Charter per book; maintain a series master; let a named checkpoint snapshot "the rules as of Draft 3."

## 5. Tensions & Traps — where the research pushes *against* Strop's stance

**T1 — Zero-question-open vs. the agreement wanting a seed.** The agreement works best with an upfront "general note describing your idiosyncrasies" ([SFWA](https://www.sfwa.org/2009/06/23/a-writers-guide-to-understanding-the-copyeditor/)), but Strop forbids blocking questions and "settings are apologies." A born-empty agreement means day-one Strop flags intentional comma splices as if they were errors — the wall it exists to avoid. **Hold the line:** born empty + infer-and-ratify, and *don't nag stylistic rules at all by default* (police problems, not style). **Bend knowingly:** offer an *optional, skippable, post-landing* "tell me a quirk" affordance — never a gate.

**T2 — Diagnosis-only vs. the writer who is stuck and wants the fix.** The data say weaker writers gain most from AI's individual-quality lift ([Doshi & Hauser](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.adn5290)), and diagnosis-only withholds exactly that. **Hold the line:** supplying text *is* the anchor that homogenizes; the support lives in question quality and scaffolding (re-entry, checkpoints, fork), not in prose. Validated by [Critical Inker](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.07167): questions produced "more substantive revisions" and less reliance than suggestions. **Never** add a "suggested rewrite" field — that one feature reintroduces the precise mechanism the research blames for diversity loss.

**T3 — Anti-homogenization vs. learning the author (the comfort bubble).** Adapting to a writer's preferences is itself a documented homogenizer/filter-bubble ([Personalisation within bounds](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2303.05453)). **Hold the line:** inference only ever *suppresses*, is auditable and reversible, and stale rules are resurfaced. The agreement is a glass box, never a black box.

**T4 — Zero-latency in-flow vs. the *beneficial* latency of human editing.** Instant local re-diagnosis removes the reflective distance and the natural stopping points ([Jane Friedman](https://janefriedman.com/before-you-hire-a-developmental-editor-what-you-need-to-know/)). **Hold the line:** deliberately throttle — batch into passes; re-diagnosis costs an author action. Treat pacing as a product stance, not a perf limitation.

**T5 — One-problem-card vs. the developmental letter.** A whole-manuscript read is by nature *not* one-span-one-problem, and risks becoming a new wall or drifting prescriptive. **Hold the line:** strengths-first, a *small* number, framed as roadmap-questions naming effect, never prose ([Fawkes](https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2019/03/how-i-write-editorial-letter-or.html)).

**T6 — Behavioral mode-inference vs. the cost of a false positive.** If Strop guesses "reviewing" during a drafting pause and fires a card mid-thought, it commits the cardinal sin. **Hold the line:** default the ambiguous state to **quiet**; always offer manual override; ship the *manual* Draft/Review toggle first and treat inference as an enhancement. A missed surfacing is cheap; a wrongful interruption is the one unforgivable error.

**T7 — "Score against nothing" vs. the writer who wants to know where they stand.** Diagnostic rivals ship dashboards because writers ask "is this any good?" ([AutoCrit](https://kindlepreneur.com/autocrit-review/)). **Hold the line:** the reassurance is strengths-first letters and the *own-baseline* voice trajectory — never a percentile against a published corpus, because benchmarking toward the published mean *is* the quiet homogenization vector.

## Dossiers (by dimension)


### core-loop

_A real writing session is not a tidy pipeline but a recursive oscillation between two fundamentally different cognitive modes: GENERATING (planning + drafting) and EVALUATING (re-reading + revising). The single most-repeated piece of craft wisdom across Lamott, King, Elbow, and Murray is that running the evaluator during generation is the cardinal sin — it kills momentum, induces block, and (per Elbow) literally "damps out the voice." Empirical work backs this: writing happens in bursts punctuated by pauses (Chenoweth & Hayes), block is caused by premature editing and rigid rules (Rose), and expert revision is meaning-level "re-seeing" while novices do surface word-swapping (Sommers). The loop is most fragile at two seams: STARTING (Resistance/avoidance, acute for ADHD task-initiation) and RE-ENTRY the next day (where the warm-up re-read becomes an editing trap). For Strop, the operative unit is not a linear stage wizard but MODE: diagnosis must be silent during drafting bursts, surface only in a review mode, be ordered by altitude (developmental -> line -> copy), and — being diagnosis-only and question-shaped — must never average the author's voice toward the mean, which is exactly the failure Elbow warns about._


**Findings**

- **The writing loop is recursive, not linear — planning, drafting (translating), and reviewing interleave under a 'monitor' and embed inside one another. So the meaningful distinction Strop should model is not sequential PHASES but the two modes of attention (generate vs evaluate) that the writer toggles between.** — Flower & Hayes' empirical think-aloud research (5 years of protocols) found the three processes are 'non-linear and highly embedded' — 'any process or sub-process can be embedded within any other.' A stage model that forces linear completion misrepresents real composing. INFERENCE: a rigid drafting->editing wizard would fight how writers actually work; modes (which can recur) are the right abstraction. [Flower & Hayes Cognitive Process Theory of Writing](https://www.yomu.ai/blog/flower-and-hayes-cognitive-process-theory-of-writing) [Flower & Hayes, 'A Cognitive Process Theory of Writing' (notes)](https://joesprelimnotes.wordpress.com/2016/03/13/flower-hayes-a-cognitive-process-theory-of-writing/)
- **The cardinal craft rule across every major source: separate drafting from editing. Mixing them is THE momentum-and-voice killer. Elbow goes furthest — 'compulsive, premature editing... makes writing dead. Your voice is damped out by all the interruptions... your voice is the main source of power.' This is the empirical/craft foundation for Strop's anti-homogenization thesis: even SELF-editing during drafting flattens voice, so an AI that rewrites would be strictly worse.** — Lamott ('Shitty First Drafts', Bird by Bird): get it all down before editing. King: write draft 1 'with the door closed' (just for you), draft 2 'with the door open.' Elbow (Writing Without Teachers, 1973): the problem 'is that editing goes on at the same time as producing'; premature editing 'makes writing dead' and damps the voice. Practitioner essays echo it: 'Writing and editing are two very different functions—one shouldn't pollute the other.' [Anne Lamott, 'Shitty First Drafts' (Bird by Bird, PDF)](https://wrd.as.uky.edu/sites/default/files/1-Shitty%20First%20Drafts.pdf) [Stephen King's Writing Process (door closed / door open)](https://www.shortform.com/blog/stephen-kings-writing-process/) [Peter Elbow, 'Freewriting' (Writing Without Teachers, PDF)](https://www.research.ucsb.edu/sites/default/files/RD/docs/FREEWRITING-by-Peter-Elbow.pdf) [4 Reasons Authors Shouldn't Edit While They Write](https://www.goodreads.com/author_blog_posts/6202795-4-reasons-authors-shouldn-t-edit-while-they-write)

Experts revise the whole and its argument; novices do 'thesaurus-philosophy' lexical tinkering because they don't see revision as developing ideas. - **Editing is not one activity — it is a stack with a mandatory order (developmental -> line -> copy -> proof) and three distinct artifacts: the editorial LETTER (whole-manuscript, big picture), MARGIN NOTES (local, scene/sentence), and the STYLE SHEET (the author's consistency decisions). Strop today is essentially margin notes only; it is missing the editorial-letter altitude and the style sheet.** — Industry sources: developmental = story/character/structure/pace (whole manuscript, early); line = sentence-level style/flow/voice; copy = grammar/spelling/consistency. Doing them in order 'prevents wasted effort, for example paying to copyedit passages that will later be cut during a structural revision.' - **Writers lose momentum most reliably at two points: STARTING (Resistance/avoidance) and when the internal critic fires too early. Block is caused not by lack of skill but by premature editing and rigid, self-imposed rules. This validates Strop's 'zero blocking questions at open' principle and demands that evaluation be absent during the vulnerable generative phase.** — Pressfield (The War of Art): 'Resistance' (fear, self-doubt, procrastination, perfectionism) is what stands between the writer and the work; the counter is simply beginning. - **Stopping and re-entry are a designed handoff, and the most common re-entry ritual — re-reading yesterday's pages — is also the most common TRAP: it slides the writer into editing mode and stalls new words. Hemingway's countermeasure (stop mid-momentum, knowing what comes next) is a re-entry affordance Strop can support directly.** — Hemingway: 'always stop when you are going good and when you know what will happen next... you will never be stuck'; this makes next-day re-entry frictionless. But warm-up guidance shows the re-read habit is double-edged: writers describe their warm-up as 'reading and editing the work done the day before' and one as 'backing up and getting a running start' — re-immersion easily becomes premature line-editing. INFERENCE: Strop needs a re-entry that re-immerses without inviting edits (e.g., a 'where you stopped / what happens next' marker and a read-only re-entry view). [Hemingway's daily output and 'stop when you know what happens next'](https://experteditor.com.au/article/m-ernest-hemingways-daily-writing-target-was-just-500-words-and-he-made-a-point-of-stopping-each-session-when-he-knew-what-would-happen-next/) [Hemingway's Routine (fs.blog)](https://fs.blog/hemingways-routine/) [How a Writing Warm-Up Routine Can Help You Write Better](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/warming-up/)
- **Flow requires clear goals, immediate feedback, challenge-skill balance, and loss of self-consciousness — which creates a sharp paradox for an AI editor: feedback supports flow, but EVALUATIVE feedback during drafting destroys the self-consciousness-free state. The resolution is that drafting wants 'progress' feedback (words, momentum), never 'judgment' feedback. The Most Dangerous Writing App is the extreme expression: it deletes your text if you pause, weaponizing 'no evaluation' to force flow.** — Csikszentmihalyi: flow 'appears when clear goals, immediate feedback, and total concentration all line up,' with self-consciousness disappearing. - **Empirically, text is produced in BURSTS separated by pauses, and fluency = longer bursts with fewer revision episodes. Keystroke research even separates production bursts (P-bursts) from revision bursts (R-bursts). This gives Strop a behavioral signal: it can infer 'drafting' (rapid P-bursts) vs 'reviewing' (pauses, scrolling, cursor jumps) from the keystroke stream and suppress cards during bursts — exactly the data Strop already captures for its keystroke-level history.** — A burst = 'a group of words... bounded by breaks' (pause ≥2s or a grammatical discontinuity from revising). Keystroke analyses distinguish P-bursts, R-bursts, and immediate-revision bursts. INFERENCE: Strop's keystroke log is sufficient to detect mode without a manual toggle. [Chenoweth & Hayes, 'Fluency in Writing' (2001)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0741088301018001004) [An analysis of pause placement in bursts of writing](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-026-06683-5)
- **The ADHD-minded writer (Strop's stated target) is defined by a task-INITIATION deficit plus hyperfocus — and is disproportionately harmed by premature-editing perfectionism and by walls of corrections that trigger shame and avoidance. This sharpens 'enough support, not neutrality': lower the start cost, protect hyperfocus once it ignites, and never present a red-underline minefield.** — Executive dysfunction in ADHD: people 'may want to do something but can't press the start button'; hyperfocus means 'once they start, they won't stop' and can be leveraged. **Pain points**

- Can't stop fiddling with the last paragraph — the internal editor short-circuits the creative surge and the draft never reaches 'the end.'
- Grammar/spell checkers underline prose in real time during drafting, breaking the burst and pulling the writer out of flow into evaluation mode.
- The daily warm-up re-read of yesterday's pages turns into an editing spiral, so the session produces zero new words.
- Facing a cold blank page each morning (task-initiation Resistance, acute for ADHD writers) — the hardest moment of the day with no on-ramp.
- Wasting effort copyediting sentences that later get cut, because surface fixes were done before structural revision (wrong-order editing).
- Fear of losing work or of an irreversible bad change makes writers cling to text and over-edit instead of cutting boldly or drafting loosely.
- Always-on feedback tools flatten idiosyncratic voice toward 'correct,' averaged prose — the writer ends up sounding like everyone else.
- Tools give a stream of line-level nags but no big-picture, whole-manuscript developmental read — no sense of altitude or priority among problems.
- A wall of simultaneous corrections (red everywhere) triggers shame and avoidance, feeding the very Resistance that stops writers from continuing.

**Tool landscape**

- **Scrivener (Composition Mode + Corkboard outlining)** — Explicit ritual containers: black-out Composition Mode + Focus/typewriter scrolling for drafting; corkboard/index cards for planning. _Gap:_ No editorial feedback or diagnosis at all; the drafting/editing/planning mode switch is entirely manual; nothing knows when you've shifted from generating to evaluating.
- **iA Writer (Focus Mode, syntax highlight)** — Radically minimal canvas; dims all but the current sentence to protect flow; optional parts-of-speech highlighting. _Gap:_ Highlighting is surface/grammatical, not structural; offers zero developmental or line-level diagnosis and no concept of revision altitude or order.
- **The Most Dangerous Writing App / Flowstate / Cold Turkey Writer** — Weaponized anti-evaluation: deletes text if you pause; Hardcore Mode hides text and disables backspace to forbid mid-draft editing. _Gap:_ Pure generation with no path to revision — it throws work away and has no editing/diagnosis layer at all; useful only as a drafting ritual.
- **Grammarly / ProWritingAid** — Always-on, real-time surface correction (grammar, style, readability) streamed as you type. _Gap:_ Fires during drafting (breaks bursts/flow), operates at copy/line altitude only, auto-suggests rewrites that average voice toward the mean — the exact homogenization Strop opposes; no mode awareness.
- **Hemingway App** — Color-coded readability scoring (sentence complexity, adverbs, passive voice) with prescriptive 'fix' suggestions. _Gap:_ Prescriptive and voice-flattening (penalizes deliberate stylistic choices); no developmental altitude; no awareness of whether you're drafting or revising.
- **Save the Cat / beat-sheet & index-card outliners** — Structured planning surface — 15 beats / ~40 scene cards to 'see the movie before writing.' _Gap:_ Planning artifact lives apart from the live draft; no feedback loop tying structural intent to the prose as it's drafted or revised.

**Strop implications**

- Ship an explicit DRAFTING MODE that suppresses ALL diagnosis — no margin cards, no underlines, no badges. Cards are computed silently and queued; they only surface when the author enters a REVIEW mode. This single gate (protecting the burst/flow window) is the most important core-loop affordance and the clearest embodiment of King's 'door closed' draft.
- Infer mode from behavior using the keystroke stream Strop already records: rapid production bursts (P-bursts) => drafting => stay silent; sustained pauses + scrolling + cursor jumps => reviewing => allowed to surface cards. Always offer a manual override, and default the ambiguous state to 'quiet.' (Grounded in Chenoweth & Hayes burst research.)
- Order diagnosis cards by ALTITUDE and enforce the industry sequence: developmental/structural questions first, line-level second, copy/grammar last. Actively suppress copy-level nags while higher-altitude problems are open — this avoids Sommers' 'thesaurus-philosophy' novice trap and prevents wasted polishing of text that may be cut.
- Add an editorial-LETTER altitude distinct from margin cards: one whole-manuscript diagnostic note (the big-picture read), mirroring how real editors pair an editorial letter with margin notes. Strop is currently margin-notes-only; this is a missing tier of the editing stack.
- Introduce a STYLE SHEET as the author's own consistency ledger (spellings, hyphenation, character/world facts) — surfaced as the author's recorded decisions, never auto-enforced. It fits the diagnosis-first ethos: Strop can flag a deviation as a question, but the fix stays the author's.
- Build a RE-ENTRY affordance for the next-day seam: a persistent 'where you stopped / what happens next' note (Hemingway's trick) plus a read-only re-immersion view so the warm-up re-read re-grounds the writer WITHOUT inviting the editing spiral that kills morning momentum.
- Lean into checkpoints/version history as core-loop psychology, not just data safety: named checkpoints (e.g., 'end of draft 1') make it emotionally safe to write a shitty first draft and to make bold structural cuts, dissolving the perfectionism that causes premature editing and block (Rose, Pressfield).
- Treat diagnosis-only + question-shaped cards as the literal answer to Elbow's warning that premature editing 'damps out the voice': a tool that rewrites would flatten voice even more than self-editing does. Never auto-apply, never average — this is Strop's defensible core, not a constraint to relax.
- For the ADHD-minded writer: minimize task-initiation cost (zero blocking questions at open — keep it), protect hyperfocus once a burst starts (no interruptions), and present ONE card at a time, dismissed-forever — never a wall of red, which triggers shame/avoidance and feeds Resistance.
- Model MODES (generate vs evaluate), not a linear stage wizard. Flower & Hayes show the loop is recursive and embedded; the writer will swing back to drafting after reviewing, so Strop's mode must be a fluid, reversible state the author re-enters many times per session, not a one-way pipeline.


### versioning

_Writers keep old versions for three distinct reasons that tools conflate: existential fear of loss (the Hemingway-suitcase trauma), the need for psychological permission to cut ("exile your darlings, don't kill them" — the morgue/cuts file), and the need to recover after a revision that drained the life out of the prose. Crucially, when a writer backtracks they are usually NOT hunting one deleted sentence (the easy, local case that diffs already serve); they are trying to recover a non-local thing — a mood, an energy, the "version where it was still alive," or a structure abandoned several drafts ago. Every mainstream tool answers "what changed and when" with a wall of timestamps and red/blue diffs, but writers backtrack asking "where was this still good and why did I change it" — a semantic question the chronology cannot answer. Manual snapshots (Scrivener) fail because they demand foresight at exactly the wrong moment; autosave histories (Word/Docs) bury meaning under arbitrary time-blocks; git-for-writers repeatedly fails on conceptual overhead; and the folk system — novel.final.FINAL.v2(real).docx — is a memory prosthesis that collapses into "eight files all called Final." Strop's keystroke-level CRDT history plus named checkpoints and voice-drift stylometry are positioned to attack precisely the gap everyone else leaves: navigating history by meaning and voice rather than by clock._


**Findings**

- **The deepest reason writers hoard versions is loss-trauma, not reuse: the archetype is Hemingway's 1922 suitcase, where his wife lost every manuscript and carbon copy at once, never recovered. This is the emotional bedrock under all backup/version behavior, even though digital autosave has largely eliminated catastrophic loss.** — Hemingway lost 'every single one of his novel manuscripts and copies' in one stolen valise; never found, never rewritten. The loss is cited as the canonical writer-nightmare. INFERENCE: digital tools removed the real risk but not the inherited fear, so writers keep hoarding versions defensively. [Hemingway, a Lost Suitcase, and the Recipe for Stupidity — Farnam Street](https://fs.blog/hemingway-suitcase/) [Ernest Hemingway lost suitcase — The Lost Media Wiki](https://lostmediawiki.com/Ernest_Hemingway_(lost_suitcase_of_manuscripts_and_early_works_from_American_novelist;_1922))
- **The cuts/morgue/darlings file is emotional scaffolding, not an asset store. Its real function is to grant permission to cut ruthlessly — 'exile your darlings, don't kill them.' Most exiled material never returns; the rare resurrection (one writer: a passage cut from page 7 reappeared on page 394) is the exception that justifies keeping the whole graveyard.** — Schaubert: separating cut text into a 'morgue' file 'transforms deletion from destruction into deferral,' making aggressive cutting psychologically possible; documents a fragment returning ~387 pages later. Editors recommend a file labeled 'darlings,' 'discards,' or 'the morgue.' INFERENCE: the value is the permission, not the recovered words. [Don't Kill Your Darlings. Exile Them. — Lance Schaubert](http://lanceschaubert.org/2015/02/19/dont-kill-your-darlings-exile-them/) [Revision: How to Kill Your Darlings — The Write Practice](https://thewritepractice.com/kill-your-darlings/)
- **A major backtracking trigger is over-revision that kills vitality: writers polish 'one more time' until the life is wrung out, then revert to an earlier draft. What they want back is the earlier version's ENERGY, not its correctness — the opposite of what a diff (which highlights surface text changes) shows.** — Calonne (in Horning, Revision): a writer 'wrung all the life out of it through too much tinkering... and then had to go back to an earlier version, anyway'; cites Auden as evidence revision 'may not always improve.' Practitioner echo: a craft post literally titled 'Wait – This Version is Worse Than the Last One!' [Creative Writers and Revision — David Stephen Calonne (WAC Clearinghouse, Horning ch.9)](https://wacclearinghouse.org/books/horning_revision/chapter9.pdf) [Wait – This Version is Worse Than the Last One! — Mike Jastrzebski's Blog](https://www.goodreads.com/author_blog_posts/637316-wait-this-version-is-worse-than-the-last-one)
- **Version history reveals WHAT changed but not WHY it mattered — the core failure. A timestamp list (or even a perfect diff) cannot tell the writer whether a revision achieved its purpose or which version held the quality they're chasing. This is the precise gap between what tools give and what backtracking writers need.** — Benjamin Miller (MTSU pedagogy text): version history shows operations (add/subtract/substitute/reorder) but diffs 'cannot prove whether revisions actually achieved their intended purposes'; cites Lindenman et al. that students claimed revision skill they 'did not enact.' Version NOTES (not timestamps) are what let writers 'recover the momentum.' [What's the Diff? Version History and Revision Reflections — The Ask (MTSU Pressbooks)](https://mtsu.pressbooks.pub/engl1020/chapter/version-history/)
- **When a second draft 'feels like a step back,' the thing the writer wants to recover is often confidence and forward momentum, not the old text — backtracking is sometimes emotional self-soothing. Treating every revert impulse as a literal request for old words misreads it.** — Janice Hardy: a messy second draft 'doesn't mean you got it wrong'; the felt 'step back' is heightened self-awareness, and what's actually lost is 'the emotional high'/forward momentum, not draft quality. INFERENCE: a versioning tool that only offers text-restore answers the wrong need here. [When the Second Draft Feels Like a Step Back — Writers in the Storm](https://writersinthestormblog.com/2025/06/when-the-second-draft-feels-like-a-step-back/)
- **Comparing your old draft against your current one is structurally biased against your earlier (possibly better) self: existing text carries an unearned 'authority and vividness' that dominates the comparison. The writer cannot fairly judge which version is better because recency and the sunk cost of the words on the page anchor them to the present draft.** — Emma Darwin: 'existing text inevitably has an authority and vividness which will tend to dominate text which doesn't yet exist'; she advises forcing existing words to 're-apply all over again.' Woolf's 'wet brush over the whole' (copy-type, never copy-paste) is invoked to defeat this anchoring. INFERENCE: comparison UIs that present current-as-default amplify the bias. [Itchy Bitesized 32: Taming Your Draft — Emma Darwin (This Itch of Writing)](https://emmadarwin.substack.com/p/itchy-bitesized-32-taming-your-draft)
- **Manual snapshots fail on a timing paradox: you must snapshot BEFORE editing, but you only realize you needed one AFTER the edit went wrong. Scrivener has no 'revert' (autosave overwrites), so the snapshot IS the only safety net — and it depends on foresight writers rarely have. The mitigation ('take snapshot on manual save') exists but is off by default and easy to miss.** — Literature & Latte: 'since Scrivener does automatic saves... there is no revert option,' so the recommended use is to snapshot 'before editing'; a separate setting 'take snapshots... on manual save' automates it but must be enabled. **Pain points**

- At the moment of backtracking, the writer faces a wall of timestamps that says nothing about why any version mattered — they have to open and read versions one by one to find 'the one where it was still good.'
- They want to recover a mood/energy/aliveness or an abandoned structure, but every tool only offers them text-restore and word-level diffs, which serve the easy single-sentence case and fail the hard non-local case.
- Manual snapshots require remembering to checkpoint BEFORE an edit goes wrong; the need is only felt AFTER, so the safety net isn't there when reached for.
- Naming/annotating versions (Google Docs named versions, Figma descriptions) requires discipline in the moment of flow that writers don't have, so versions stay unlabeled and meaningless.
- Comparing old vs new to decide which is better is biased: the current draft's words have unearned 'authority,' so the writer can't fairly judge whether their earlier self was right.
- The cuts/darlings file lives outside the manuscript as a loose document; cut material is hard to search, easy to lose, and disconnected from where it was removed — so the 'permission to cut' it was meant to grant erodes.
- Folk versioning (final.FINAL.v2.docx, dated copies) collapses into chaos: 'eight files all called Final,' no reliable order, no way to find what you're looking for.
- Reverting feels like a scary, destructive commit (especially where there's no undo after restart), so writers make defensive duplicate files instead of trusting the tool — multiplying the chaos.
- Track-changes-style review overwhelms with zillions of granular edits and a serial accept/reject burden, conflating 'what to change' with 'what this used to be.'
- Git and other developer version control impose foreign concepts and plain-text constraints, so the one rigorous solution is inaccessible to the people who'd benefit.

**Tool landscape**

- **Scrivener Snapshots** — Manual per-document checkpoints in the Inspector side panel; red-strikethrough/blue-underline compare; Roll Back offers to snapshot current state first; optional 'snapshot on manual save.' _Gap:_ Foresight paradox — you must snapshot before editing but realize the need after; off-by-default automation; per-document not project-wide; no continuous history, so anything between snapshots is unrecoverable.
- **Google Docs / Word (OneDrive) Version History** — Automatic time-grouped snapshots in a right panel; per-author colored inline diff (Docs); optional named versions (up to ~40); read-only takeover to preview a version. _Gap:_ A wall of arbitrary time-blocks that shows what/when, never why; named versions require discipline writers lack; meaning is buried; no way to navigate by quality, voice, or mood.
- **Track Changes (Word/Docs)** — Inline redline or margin balloons capturing every edit as accept/reject units during review. _Gap:_ Overwhelms with 'zillions' of granular changes; serial accept/reject burden; designed for reviewing OTHERS, not for personally recovering a past state; conflates review with version history.
- **git-for-writers experiments** — Commits, branches, diffs over plain-text manuscripts; rigorous, lossless, every revision reachable. _Gap:_ Conceptual overhead and foreign vocabulary; plain-text-only; benefits invisible upfront; repeatedly attempted and repeatedly abandoned by non-technical writers.
- **Folk system: novel.final.FINAL.v2(real).docx / dated copies** — Manual file duplication with semantic-ish names as a memory prosthesis for why a version matters. _Gap:_ Collapses into chaos ('eight files all called Final'); unreliable ordering; no diff; the very metadata it tries to encode is fragile and quickly meaningless.
- **Figma named versions (cross-domain best case)** — Autosave checkpoints collapsed under named versions with Title + Description; restore inserts paired checkpoints so nothing is lost; ~30-min auto-checkpoints. _Gap:_ Still relies on humans to name versions; description is free-text communication metadata, not derived meaning; designed for design files/stakeholders, not prose mood/voice.
- **Apple Pages / macOS Versions** — Full-screen Time Machine takeover: current doc vs stack of past versions, tick-mark timeline, copy/paste across versions. _Gap:_ Purely visual thumbnail comparison with NO diff; disconnected full-screen mode; works for layout, poor for prose where the change is in the words, not the picture.

**Strop implications**

- Strop's keystroke-level CRDT history that survives restart is the structural answer to BOTH the Hemingway fear-of-loss and Scrivener's foresight paradox: nothing is ever lost and the author never has to remember to snapshot. Market and design it as 'you can never lose a word, and you never have to decide in advance to keep one' — the anti-snapshot.
- Attack the wall-of-timestamps directly: a chronological list is the documented failure of every competitor. Strop's named checkpoints and voice-drift sparkline should make history navigable by MEANING and VOICE, not clock — let the author scrub to 'where the voice was strongest' or 'before the flattening,' a trajectory read no timestamp list can give.
- Marry the diagnosis engine to history. A checkpoint should carry the diagnosis cards that were open at that moment, so a version is labeled by the PROBLEMS it had/answered ('this is the version before I over-controlled the dialogue'), turning the timeline into the semantic why-layer everyone else omits — and doing it by derivation, not by demanding the author name versions (settings are apologies).
- Make the morgue/cuts file a first-class, in-product surface, not a loose sidecar doc. When the author cuts text (especially in response to a diagnosis card), the words go to a searchable 'exile' drawer anchored to where they were removed — giving the psychological permission-to-cut that ruthless revision needs, consistent with 'the fix is always the author's.'
- Anti-homogenization guardrail for the morgue: Strop must NEVER auto-suggest reusing an exiled darling or surface it as a fix. Preservation only. Re-injecting cut material would be exactly the voice-averaging the product exists to refuse; the corpse is kept for the author to resurrect, never for the machine to recycle.
- Backtracking is usually non-local (mood/energy/structure), so a word-level diff is necessary but not sufficient. Lean on stylometry to answer the real question: surface 'these three checkpoints scored highest on your own voice' as a diagnosis-style observation, then let the author read and decide — name the trajectory, don't pick a winner.
- Defuse the comparison bias (Emma Darwin's 'existing text has authority'): when comparing old vs current, do NOT present current-as-default-winner. Frame it as a diagnosis question — 'the earlier version had more momentum here; the current is more controlled — which serves the scene?' — so the author judges their earlier self fairly. This is diagnosis-first applied to versioning.
- Make reverting non-scary and non-destructive: because history is continuous, a revert is just a new state you can leave again (Figma's paired-checkpoint safety, achieved for free). Communicate this explicitly so writers stop defensively duplicating files — kill the final.FINAL.v2 reflex by making the tool trustworthy enough to not need it.
- Respect that some revert impulses are discouragement, not a text request (the 'second draft feels like a step back' case). For an ADHD-minded writer, the history panel should make forward progress legible — a 'parade of small rewards' / momentum view — so the urge to thrash backward is met with evidence of motion, not a frictionless time machine that enables spiraling.
- Do not rebuild track-changes: avoid a serial accept/reject firehose of granular edits. Strop's one-problem-per-card, author-decides, dismissed-card-never-returns model is already the antidote to track-changes overwhelm; keep version diffs as readable inline redline for orientation, but route DECISIONS through diagnosis cards, not through a thousand toggles.


### parallel-edits

_Writers absolutely create competing versions of the same passage — not just sequential drafts, but starkly different takes held at once (Elizabeth Rusch ran five simultaneous versions of one picture book; Jennifer Crusie kept seven versions of a single breakfast scene; Max Barry seeds novels by writing many disconnected scenes and letting the best survive). The behavior splits into three real-world patterns: a "scratch zone" at the bottom of the file (the dominant idiom — 15 of 15 writers in one survey dump cuts there, calling it the "piggy bank," "Things cut," or "Candy Bag"), separate side-by-side files/snapshots, and inline alternates (which only exist in screenwriting tools — Final Draft "Alt Dialogue," Highland's Scratchpad/Bin — and have NO prose-tool equivalent). The single most important finding for Strop is the reconciliation FAILURE MODE: Crusie tried to mechanically combine her seven versions and called it "washing garbage" — the life gets washed out and the fix is to stop merging and rewrite fresh ("it's not a set of Lego blocks, it's a story; I don't assemble stories, I write them"). Current tools all force a diff/merge mental model (Word's redline "baffles" prose writers; Google Docs version history is one linear timeline built for accept/reject, not two equal live drafts; Scrivener's Roll Back is all-or-nothing and not undoable). HCI backs the upstream behavior: Dow's Stanford study shows parallel prototyping beats serial on results, divergence, and self-efficacy, precisely because critique lands on options rather than on your only baby. The prose-thinker wants to hold A and B in reading order, hear which one sings, take a line from each by retyping, and never see red/green confetti._

**Pain points**

- "Washing garbage": trying to combine multiple competing drafts line-by-line drains the life out of the prose, and the writer can't tell when to stop merging and start fresh.
- The scratch zone at the bottom of the file is improvised in every tool — writers reinvent a 'piggy bank'/'Things cut'/'morgue' by hand, and those cuttings drift out of context, disconnected from where the text used to live.
- No prose editor lets you keep Version A and Version B in place at the same spot; you must fork into separate files/snapshots, which physically separates the two options you're trying to weigh.
- Diff/redline views (Word Compare, version-diff tools) render prose changes as red/green confetti that 'baffles' prose-thinkers — they read by ear and rhythm, not by token-level adds/deletes.
- Scrivener's Roll Back / Restore is all-or-nothing and not undoable: you can't take this sentence from the old version and that sentence from the new one without manual copy-paste.
- Google Docs and Word treat versions as a single linear timeline or as someone-else's-suggestions to accept/reject — neither models two co-equal authorial drafts of the same passage held at once.
- Deleting feels final and frightening, so writers hoard everything; without a felt guarantee of recoverability they over-keep, and the hoard itself becomes paralyzing.
- 'Rewrite exhaustion': with seven versions in a folder, the writer loses track of which lines were the good ones and why, and ends up worse off than with a single confident draft.

**Tool landscape**

- **Scrivener (Snapshots + Compare + Split Editor + Duplicate)** — Manual per-document snapshots with paragraph/clause/word-level Compare highlighting; split editor to view two docs side by side; Duplicate to branch a whole document. _Gap:_ Capture is great but Compare is a diff-reader, not a weaving surface; Roll Back is all-or-nothing and not undoable; merging keepers back is pure manual copy-paste; no inline A/B at the point of use.
- **Google Docs (Suggesting mode + Version History)** — Suggestions appear in margin to accept/reject; a single linear named version history you can browse and restore. _Gap:_ Designed for collaborative accept/reject and one timeline — cannot hold two co-equal live drafts of the same passage; restoring is linear, not a pick-lines-from-each merge.
- **Microsoft Word (Compare / Track Changes redline)** — Combine/Compare two files into a marked-up redline of insertions and deletions. _Gap:_ Produces 'a chaotic mess of strike-throughs, balloons, and formatting clutter' that 'baffles' prose writers; a developer's diff model imposed on people who read by ear.
- **Final Draft 'Alt Dialogue' (screenwriting)** — Attach unlimited alternate versions to a line in place; toggle between numbered alts with arrows; nothing leaves the flow of the script. _Gap:_ Screenplay-only and dialogue-only; no equivalent for prose paragraphs/scenes; doesn't help reconcile beyond pick-one.
- **Highland 2 (Scratchpad + Bin + Boneyard)** — Scratchpad to 'play with alternate takes of a line before choosing your favorite'; Bin to trim alt lines/scenes for later review; Boneyard /omit/ to keep text inline-but-hidden. _Gap:_ Screenwriting tool; the scratchpad is a side space, not anchored A/B in the manuscript; reconciliation is still manual.
- **'Scratch zone at the bottom of the file' (the manual idiom)** — Writers dump cuts/alternates at the end of the doc ('piggy bank,' 'Things cut,' 'Candy Bag') or in a morgue/Deleted-Scenes folder; survives because cut-and-paste feels safer than delete. _Gap:_ Improvised in every tool; cuttings lose their original context/anchor; no link back to where they belonged; safety is a vibe, not a guarantee.
- **ProseEngine 'Version Diff' / Novelcrafter version history** — Named snapshots with word-level green/red diff (ProseEngine); per-scene linear version history with restore (Novelcrafter). _Gap:_ Explicitly imports the software 'version control' model; linear rollback, not parallel co-equal drafts; the diff confetti is the wrong reading model for prose decisions.

**Strop implications**

- Build 'Try it both ways' as a first-class, in-place PROSE fork: select a sentence/paragraph/scene and spawn Version A / Version B anchored at the SAME spot in the manuscript, toggled inline (the Final Draft 'Alt Dialogue' pattern, finally brought to prose). Neither version is privileged; both live in keystroke history. Use the writer's own words — 'Version A / Version B,' 'try it both ways' — never 'branch,' 'diff,' or 'merge.'
- NEVER ship an auto-merge / 'combine versions' / three-way-merge button. Crusie's 'washing garbage' is the canonical proof that mechanically stitching keeper-lines kills prose. Reconciliation in Strop must be an authorial REWRITE: the writer reads A and B, then types the final in place. Collapsing a fork picks ONE (or replaces both with a freshly typed third); the loser goes to history, never auto-woven in.
- When showing two competing versions, render them as two whole, readable passages in reading order (toggle or stacked), optimized for the read-aloud/ear test — NOT as red/green character confetti. The diff view is the diff-thinker's tool and is the wrong mental model here; reserve any inline diff strictly for the existing history panel, not for live A/B choosing.
- Give the beloved 'scratch zone' a sanctioned home: a per-document 'Cuttings'/'piggy bank' surface (a real feature, not a habit at the bottom of the file) where forked-but-not-chosen passages and cut darlings land WITH a back-link to where they came from — solving the 'cuttings lose their context' pain. Because Strop's keystroke history already means nothing is ever lost, frame it as intentional holding-for-reuse, reinforcing 'nothing can be lost, and the writer must FEEL it' (zero 'are you sure?' dialogs).
- Let diagnosis cards operate ON a fork without choosing for the writer: a card can NAME what differs ('Version A opens on the image; Version B opens on the line of dialogue — which entry point do you want the reader to land on?') and ask one question. This is the Dow parallel-prototyping insight operationalized: critique across options, author decides, voice never averaged. A card that recommended a winner would violate the diagnosis-only thesis.
- Design explicitly against 'rewrite exhaustion': make ABANDONING the pile a graceful, encouraged move. When a passage accumulates several forks, surface a gentle 'start this one fresh?' path that keeps the surrounding context (and the named diagnoses) while clearing the competing drafts to history — Crusie's 'close the folder and write it fresh' as a supported action, not a failure.
- Keep the anti-features list firm and consistent with prior history-panel research: no undo-tree/branch DAG (even programmers find them disorienting; restore-as-forward-edit linearization is correct), no side-by-side diff panes as the primary surface, no per-author colors. A discarded fork should behave like a dismissed card — retrievable from history but it never returns to nag.
- Position this as the prose-native answer to a gap NO competitor fills: Scrivener/Docs/Word force file-duplication or linear timelines; screenwriting tools have inline alts but only for scripts. Strop's local-first keystroke CRDT is uniquely suited to host multiple co-equal live drafts of one passage with full provenance — the 'try two versions, then reconcile by rewriting' flow that feels like writing, not like operating version control.


### editor-relationship

_The professional writer-editor relationship is a hierarchy of intervention — developmental (story/structure) → line (style/sentence) → copyedit (mechanics/consistency) → proofread (typos in proofs) — and, crucially, the deepest, most respected levels DIAGNOSE rather than fix. Its two canonical artifacts map almost exactly onto Strop's design: the developmental EDITORIAL LETTER (big-picture, strengths-before-concerns, a "roadmap for revision," never a rewrite) and the IN-MARGIN QUERY (one localized question anchored to specific text), backed by a third persistence artifact, the copyeditor's STYLE SHEET (a running record of every decision). The profession's cardinal rule is literally Strop's thesis: "It's not my manuscript," "thou shalt not change the author's meaning," and the best query names what the text is doing and asks "intentional?" rather than prescribing a rewrite. The emotional core is fragile — receiving edits is "a shock and a blow," authors fixate on criticism, swing between defensiveness and imposter syndrome, and fear an editor who "flattens" their voice into something "they no longer recognize." The cadence is slow and asynchronous (4+ weeks of latency per round, multiple rounds over 6-18 months), and the author's worst moment is the undifferentiated FLOOD of red track-changes markup ("dizzying," "a wall of depression"). Empirical HCI work (Cornell/CHI 2025; Doshi & Hauser) confirms that suggestion tools that rewrite homogenize voice toward a generic mean — validating Strop's diagnosis-only, never-rewrite stance as the editorial gold standard rather than a v1 limitation._


**Findings**

- **The four editing levels are a hierarchy of intervention by altitude — and the deepest, most prestigious level (developmental) deliberately does NOT touch the prose; it diagnoses via a letter. **Pain points**

- The red-markup flood: opening an edited manuscript shows 'hundreds (if not thousands) of tiny changes' in one undifferentiated colored stream — 'dizzying,' 'overwhelming and disheartening,' a 'wall of depression' — with a comma fix weighted identically to a structural problem.
- No durable decision memory: once a Word/Docs comment is resolved or accepted/rejected, the flag and the author's reasoning vanish. Across rounds there is no answer to 'what did I already decide about this, and why?' The only persistent record (the style sheet) is the editor's, not the author's.
- Vague, unanchored queries force guessing: 'AU: Revise?' with no highlighted span and no stated concern makes the author reverse-engineer what the editor objected to.
- Prescriptive rewrites erase voice: the editor (or AI tool) who substitutes their preferred wording flattens the author's register, humor, and cultural specificity — the fear of getting back 'a book they no longer recognize as their own.'
- Defensiveness has no scaffold: the author must privately self-regulate ('am I too attached to this turn of phrase?') with nothing in the tool helping separate 'there is a problem here' from 'these exact words must change.'
- Latency and batching: 4+ weeks of waiting, then all feedback arrives at once as a wall to be processed — the opposite of feedback in the flow of drafting.
- Over-querying / query fatigue: too many comments, or multi-paragraph queries that get skimmed and so fail to land at all.
- Condescension: the 'high-school English teacher' tone — rule-lecturing, scolding, 'it looks funny' corrections — instantly destroys trust, especially for a writer who 'smells condescension instantly.'

**Tool landscape**

- **MS Word — Track Changes + Comments** — Inline colored insertions/deletions plus margin comment threads; the de-facto standard for author-editor exchange. Author accepts/rejects each change. _Gap:_ Conflates all editing altitudes into one overwhelming flood; comments are ephemeral (resolved/deleted), leaving no durable record of what was flagged or what the author decided; it is fundamentally a rewrite-acceptance machine, not a diagnosis surface; offers no protection for voice.
- **Google Docs — Suggesting mode + comments** — Cloud, real-time async suggestions and threaded comments; collaborative accept/reject. _Gap:_ Same accept/reject rewrite model; resolved comments disappear; cloud-based (not local-first, text leaves the machine); no separation of diagnosis from prescription; no voice safeguard.
- **Copyeditor's style sheet (Word/Excel/PerfectIt)** — A persistent, hand-built running record of every spelling/name/hyphenation/timeline/grammar decision so choices stay consistent across a manuscript and across rounds. _Gap:_ Maintained BY the editor FOR consistency enforcement — not author-facing, not linked to the live text, not queryable; it is the one good persistence idea in the workflow but it lives outside the author's hands.
- **ProWritingAid / Grammarly (automated line/copy tools)** — Real-time automated suggestions for grammar, style, and 'clarity,' often one-click apply. _Gap:_ Prescriptive auto-fixes that average prose toward 'standard/safe/stiff'; empirically homogenize voice; no big-picture (developmental) diagnosis; they APPLY rather than ASK, and assume error rather than intent.
- **Developmental editorial letter (standalone prose document)** — The gold-standard big-picture diagnosis: strengths first, then concerns, as a revision roadmap; names problems, leaves fixes to the author. _Gap:_ Static and detached from manuscript locations — the author must manually map each letter point back to the text; no two-way anchoring; one-shot per round with weeks of latency.
- **Human developmental/line editor** — The ideal 'name the problem, ask the question, the author decides' relationship; reads for voice before intervening; builds trust through carefulness/transparency/flexibility. _Gap:_ Weeks of latency per round, expensive, one batch at a time; persistence between rounds is limited to the letter and the editor's memory; not available in the flow of drafting.

**Strop implications**

- Strop's diagnosis card IS the in-margin editorial query, formalized — so adopt the profession's good-query rules as HARD card constraints: anchored to a specific highlighted span; exactly one problem per card; a concern plus a question, never a command; reader-framed ('a reader might lose the thread here'); open-ended, not yes/no; and short — the ACES/Copyeditor's Handbook 'one or two sentences maximum, or it gets skimmed' rule should cap card length.
- Make 'this passage is doing X — is that intentional?' the canonical card grammar. It is the literal best-practice query from the copyediting canon (the Vietnam/Civil-War example) and it presupposes author competence, which is exactly Strop's 'enough support, not neutrality, for a writer who smells condescension.'
- Encode 'It's not my manuscript' / 'thou shalt not change the author's meaning' as the product's spine and reframe v1 diagnosis-only as the GOLD STANDARD, not a limitation: the most respected editorial level (developmental) already withholds the prose. A card must never carry a prefilled rewrite — 'the fix is always the author's' = the profession's 'editorial judgment + self-effacement.'
- Separate altitude to defeat the 'wall of depression.' Let cards declare a level (structural / line / mechanical) and let the author filter or collapse by level, so a comma flag is never weighted identically to 'this chapter has no stakes.' The margin must never dump 200 undifferentiated flags — pace and cap visible cards.
- Lean into the margin-card model as the anti-flood antidote: never render a track-changes diff. One card = one named problem is structurally the opposite of Word's dizzying red stream, and that contrast is a core marketing wedge.
- Reframe Strop's history + 'a dismissed card never returns' as the AUTHOR'S OWN STYLE SHEET — the durable, queryable decision log that Word/Docs structurally cannot keep. Make the history panel teachable as exactly that ('what was flagged and what you decided'), and consider surfacing recurring dismissals as learned preferences (the editor who finally learns 'this author uses ellipses on purpose').
- Add a developmental-LETTER surface distinct from per-span cards: a small number of manuscript-level diagnoses, delivered strengths-first as a roadmap rather than inline. Every editorial-letter source insists the letter opens with what's working and reads as 'a vote of confidence' — for an ADHD-minded writer, leading with strengths is what prevents the defensiveness spiral and the wall of depression.
- Use Strop's never-rewrite architecture as defensiveness scaffolding: because it can only flag the problem at the level of effect/vision, it forces the author into the mature-author move — 'my vision and my wording are not the same thing.' Card copy should name the problem as an effect ('readers may lose the thread'), never as a prose substitution.
- Make local-first / BYO-LLM the explicit answer to the voice-flattening fear. The Cornell and Doshi-Hauser homogenization findings are Strop's empirical wedge: position it as 'the editor that never averages your voice toward the mean' — the structural opposite of the autocomplete tools that make prose 'standard, safe, stiff.'
- Govern card TONE against the 'condescending English teacher' failure mode that the literature flags as instantly trust-destroying: no rule-citation lectures, no 'it looks funny' (ILF/ISF) nags, no yes/no quizzes, no scolding. This aligns with Strop's prior design decision that play lives in mechanics, never in condescending aesthetics.
- Respect the 'look it up first / don't query what you can verify' principle: Strop should suppress cards for anything it can resolve or that is mere preference, and choose its battles — over-querying is a documented way to get the whole margin ignored. Restraint (fewer, higher-confidence cards) is a feature, consistent with 'settings are apologies' and opinionated defaults.


### latency-and-ai

_Instant, unlimited, on-demand editorial feedback changes writing in ways that are now empirically documented, and most of the well-evidenced effects are negative for exactly the things Strop cares about: voice, diversity of expression, and the drafting loop. Multiple peer-reviewed studies show AI assistance averages prose toward a statistical mean — it raises individual quality (especially for weaker writers) while collectively shrinking the space of what gets written (Doshi & Hauser, Science Advances 2024; Padmakumar & He, ICLR 2024), and it flattens cultural and personal voice toward a generic Western register (Agarwal et al., 2024). The mechanism is anchoring: when a tool supplies replacement text or ideas, writers converge on it; the more it supplies, the more they converge. Separately, HCI research shows real-time suggestions interrupt drafting flow, and that high trust in AI suppresses critical thinking and erodes skill (Microsoft/CMU CHI 2025; "Ironies of Generative AI"). The robust good news for Strop: tools that diagnose and provoke rather than rewrite, that introduce deliberate friction (cognitive forcing functions), and that preserve human cognitive work do not produce these harms — and a diagnosis-only design that supplies no replacement text structurally avoids the anchoring that causes homogenization. The design challenge is pacing: instant capability must be deliberately throttled so feedback lands at revision time, not mid-draft, and never becomes an infinite tinkering slot machine._


**Findings**

- **AI assistance is a documented homogenizer: it raises individual quality while collectively narrowing the space of what gets written — a 'social dilemma' where each writer is individually better off but the corpus becomes more uniform.** — Doshi & Hauser (Science Advances, 2024), n=293 writers + 600 evaluators, 3 conditions (no AI / 1 AI idea / 5 AI ideas): AI access raised novelty 5.4–8.1% and usefulness 3.7–9.0%, but AI-assisted stories were measurably more similar to each other (~9–11% of the total similarity range) and 5% more similar to the AI ideas themselves. Padmakumar & He (ICLR 2024) independently found writing with feedback-tuned InstructGPT produced a statistically significant reduction in lexical and content diversity and increased cross-author similarity, traceable to the model contributing less-diverse text. [Generative AI enhances individual creativity but reduces the collective diversity of novel content (Science Advances)](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.adn5290) [Doshi & Hauser, open-access (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11244532/) [Padmakumar & He, Does Writing with Language Models Reduce Content Diversity? (ICLR 2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.05196)
- **The homogenization mechanism is ANCHORING/fixation on supplied content — and it scales with how much the tool gives you. **Pain points**

- Grammarly/ProWritingAid suggestions 'flatten my voice' and strip personal tone; experienced writers accept only ~half and feel they're fighting the tool to stay original.
- Inline, real-time suggestions interrupt thought mid-sentence — writers describe them as 'intrusive' and 'messing up my thought process,' and some disable them entirely.
- Feature-maximal tools (ProWritingAid runs 25+ reports) fuel perfectionism: there's always one more flag to chase, so the manuscript never feels done.
- Instant + unlimited feedback removes the natural stopping points of human editing, producing anxious tinkering and a 'never finishing' loop.
- Writers can feel their work converging on a generic, 'AI-smell' register and losing what made it theirs — often without noticing until later.
- Idiosyncrasy, dialect, deliberate register and non-Western phrasing get flagged as 'errors' and corrected toward a Western/standard mean.
- Over-trusting the tool erodes the writer's own editing instinct over time (deskilling); the more they lean on it, the less they think.
- The writer's role silently degrades from composing to babysitting/evaluating AI output, which is less engaging and lower-agency.
- Rule-based stylistic nags (passive voice, adverbs, sentence length) push every writer toward the same 'correct' style, encoding a mean into the tool itself.

**Tool landscape**

- **Grammarly** — Always-on, real-time inline corrections and rewrites optimized for clarity/correctness in business and academic registers; one-click 'accept' culture. _Gap:_ Documented to flatten creative voice and tone; speed-and-accept design maximizes the over-reliance and anchoring it should avoid for fiction/voice-driven prose.
- **ProWritingAid** — Craft-oriented analytics: 25+ reports (pacing, echoes, sticky sentences, readability) plus inline suggestions; rule-based and 'by-the-book.' _Gap:_ Report maximalism fuels perfectionism and endless tinkering; rule-based scoring flattens emotionally-charged or humorous passages because it optimizes toward a norm rather than the author's intent.
- **Sudowrite** — Fiction-first generation + 'Describe/Expand/Rewrite' and editorial-feedback tools that name pacing issues, passive voice, unclear motivation, plot holes. _Gap:_ Core value prop is generating prose (anchoring source #1 in the homogenization literature); diagnosis is a side feature, not a discipline — it will happily write the author's text for them.
- **Novelcrafter** — Power-user infrastructure: Codex/worldbuilding consistency, model-agnostic, scene-by-scene drafting and management; you wire up your own workflow. _Gap:_ Optimized for control and generation, not for protecting drafting flow or voice; prose described as 'workmanlike,' and the configuration burden is the opposite of zero-blocking-questions-at-open.
- **Lex** — Word processor with on-demand AI: '+++' continuations, brainstorming, and 'ask for feedback'; framed by its founder as a 'personal trainer' for the writer rather than a replacement. _Gap:_ Closest in spirit to diagnosis (feedback on request), but still offers generation and voice-cloning, and feedback is general/conversational rather than a disciplined one-problem-one-question margin the author adjudicates.
- **Generic LLM 'editor' (ChatGPT/Claude paste-in)** — Unlimited, instant, conversational critique-and-rewrite; the writer pastes text and asks 'make this better.' _Gap:_ Strongest anchoring/homogenization risk and highest automation-bias surface; no pacing, no flow protection, no persistence of decisions, no voice memory, text leaves the machine — and it defaults to rewriting, not diagnosing.

**Strop implications**

- Diagnosis-only is anti-homogenization BY CONSTRUCTION, and Strop should say so loudly: the empirical convergence comes from anchoring on supplied text/ideas (Doshi & Hauser; Padmakumar & He). Because Strop emits a named problem and a question but never replacement prose, there is no 'mean' for the author to drift toward. Never add a 'suggested rewrite,' 'better version,' or example sentence to a card — that single feature would reintroduce the exact anchor the research blames for diversity loss.
- Pace to the writing loop, not to the model's latency: instant capability must be deliberately throttled. Detect active drafting (sustained typing) and stay SILENT — no cards, no margin churn — surfacing diagnosis only on a pause, on scroll-back, on explicit request, or in an explicit 'revision' mode. This is backed both by HCI flow-interruption findings and by the universal craft rule to separate drafting from editing (and even 'turn the grammar checker off while drafting').
- Batch diagnosis into discrete PASSES, not a live keystroke stream. Recreate the beneficial reflective distance of the editorial-letter cadence: a finite set of cards the author can work through and then dismiss, rather than an always-updating wall. The human editor's latency is partly a feature; Strop should simulate its rhythm, not erase it.
- Make the margin scarce on purpose — a small budget of cards per view. ProWritingAid's 25-report maximalism is a cautionary tale: more flags fuel perfectionism and the 'never finishing' loop. 'Settings are apologies' applies to card volume too — opinionated triage (the few problems that matter now) beats exhaustive coverage.
- 'Dismissed card never returns' is not just UX courtesy — it is a cognitive forcing function and the off-ramp from infinite tinkering. It forces the author to adjudicate (countering automation bias: the Microsoft/CMU finding is that trust in AI lowers critical thinking) and it guarantees a finite, completable backlog. Keep dismissal frictionless and permanent.
- Hold cards to a non-authoritative, uncertain VOICE. Since high confidence in the AI predicts less author thinking, cards should read as one editor's reading — 'this might be flat; does it land for you?' — never as a verdict. The frame that protects both voice and skill is a question the author answers, not a defect the author clears.
- Police PROBLEMS, not STYLE. The homogenizing checkers flag passive voice, adverbs, and sentence length — encoding a mean into the tool. Strop's diagnosis taxonomy should target things only the author can adjudicate (contradiction, unearned emotion, buried lede, structural confusion, unclear motivation) and explicitly refuse to nag on stylistic 'rules,' because rule-nags are the mechanism of flattening.
- Be voice-aware and idiosyncrasy-protecting. The Western-flattening result (classifier accuracy 90.6%->83.5%) means a naive 'deviation from norm = error' detector will sand off dialect, register, and personality. Strop should let the author declare voice/intent (an editor's style sheet) and should actively flag-and-PROTECT what is distinctive ('this is your strongest voice on the page — keep it'), borrowing the 'diverse-personas preserve diversity' finding as a design goal.
- Use checkpoints/version history as the explicit cure for tinkering-forever. Named checkpoints + restart-surviving undo let an ADHD-minded writer try a fix and roll back without fear, replacing the editor's 'rounds' with author-owned milestones. Consider a gentle nudge — 'you've reworked this paragraph many times; checkpoint and move on?' — to convert anxious looping into a decision.
- Don't stream just because local/BYO-LLM makes it cheap. With Ollama the marginal cost of constant re-diagnosis is ~zero, which is precisely the trap — unlimited instant feedback is what creates the anxiety/slot-machine loop. Treat pacing as a deliberate product stance: re-diagnosis should cost a deliberate author action, not happen automatically on every keystroke.
- Keep friction lightweight for the ADHD writer. Buçinca's forcing functions worked but were liked less and helped high-Need-for-Cognition users most. Strop's friction (decide-on-each-card) must stay skimmable, optional, and never blocking — consistent with 'enough support, not neutrality' and 'land in the document with zero blocking questions at open.'


### editorial-agreement

_The copyeditor's style sheet is the closest existing artifact to Strop's proposed "editorial agreement," and it already does double duty: half of it is a FACTUAL CANON the editor must enforce (name spellings, character traits, timeline, hyphenation decisions), and half is a LEAVE-ALONE CONTRACT that tells the editor what NOT to touch (voice/register notes, "comma splices are integral to my rhythm," dialect, intentional fragments). It is explicitly described in the trade as a "living document," built primarily by the copyeditor through inference ("like a detective reconstructing motivations from clues") but seeded by author-supplied lists and a "general note describing your idiosyncrasies of style"—so the real-world answer to "explicit vs inferred" is decisively BOTH, with the crucial detail that the inferred sheet is always surfaced back to the author for confirmation, never silent. Writers today communicate boundaries through stet ("let it stand"), three dots under intentional rule-breaks, and pre-edit conversations; the governing virtue is the copyeditor's "fine sense of when to leave things alone"—which is precisely Strop's thesis. The dominant failure mode, confirmed across linter-suppression culture and recommender filter-bubble research, is that a tunable suppression contract degrades into a way to silence all useful criticism; the engineering world's countermeasures (ban blanket disables, require a named target plus a reason, flag stale/unused suppressions) translate directly into Strop guardrails. For Strop specifically: the agreement should be born empty (zero blocking questions at open), grow by surfacing inferred patterns for one-tap confirmation rather than silent learning, keep a per-instance dismissal distinct from a standing rule, and split itself into a Continuity Canon (which ENABLES new diagnosis cards) and a Voice Charter (which SUPPRESSES them)._

**Pain points**

- A per-instance 'no thanks' has no clean way to become a standing rule: writers fear that if they stet one comma splice the editor (human or tool) will keep flagging the next forty identical ones, and Writer Unboxed advice is literally to 'stet the first change and add a comment explaining you'd like a global change' — manual, repetitive, easy to miss.
- Writers feel interrogated when every choice they've made 'will be called into question... right down to where every last punctuation mark is laid'; receiving a wall of flags triggers an emotional reaction ('most writers bristle when told our work has been found lacking') before any judgment is possible.
- The boundary-communication channel is lossy and front-loaded: the 'general note describing your idiosyncrasies' must be written before the edit, from memory, when the author often hasn't yet consciously noticed their own patterns ('the preferred style dominates only toward the end').
- Style sheets stagnate — the #1 listed pitfall — because updating them is manual busywork detached from the act of writing; the living artifact dies the moment maintenance competes with drafting.
- Series drift: keeping a master sheet consistent across multiple books is purely manual, and continuity errors (eye color, who-knows-what-when) slip through because the bible isn't connected to the text.
- Authors can't easily tell whether an editor is 'making appropriate corrections or crushing your creative spirit' — there's no transparency into WHY a thing was flagged, forcing a request for context after the fact.
- The blunt instruments today are all-or-nothing: 'if you really feel nothing should be changed' your only move is a global do-not-touch note that also suppresses genuine inadvertencies — exactly the blanket-disable that hides real errors.

**Tool landscape**

- **Copyeditor's style sheet (analog gold standard)** — A human-maintained living document split into general-style/characters/places/timeline plus a voice note; built by the editor through inference, seeded by author lists, handed to proofreader and reused for the series. _Gap:_ Lives in a Word/Google doc disconnected from any flagging engine; not machine-actionable; depends entirely on one editor's 'fine sense' and dies if not manually updated each pass. Strop can make the same artifact executable.
- **Grammarly (rule toggles + personal dictionary + dismiss)** — Explicit: 'Turn off suggestions like this,' deactivate suggestion groups at account.grammarly.com, add words to a personal dictionary. Implicit: per-suggestion Accept/Dismiss. _Gap:_ Cloud-based (violates Strop's local-first/text-never-leaves-machine path); preferences are account-global, not per-manuscript or per-series; no voice charter in the author's own words; no continuity canon; learns little from dismiss history and never explains a flag's rationale.
- **Series bible (Bransford / River / Tasha Harrison)** — A standalone reference doc for cross-book continuity — character profiles, worldbuilding rules, chronology, naming/formatting conventions — shared with editors to keep everyone aligned. _Gap:_ Manual, drifts, and is never enforced against the prose; it's a passive memory, not an active checker. Strop could make a series-master that actually catches violations.
- **Linters (ESLint/Oxlint/Ruff suppression model)** — Inline suppression with named-rule targeting, plus governance rules: 'no-abusive-eslint-disable' bans blanket silencing, best practice requires a reason comment, and report-unused-disable-directives surfaces stale suppressions. _Gap:_ Built for deterministic code, not voice; suppression is binary on/off with no notion of 'this character on purpose'; no voice notes. But its anti-abuse governance is the best existing template for keeping an editorial agreement honest.
- **Developmental editorial letter / manuscript evaluation** — A diagnosis-first prose artifact: names big-picture problems and poses questions ('does the protagonist hold agency across all acts?'), explicitly states the author is 'under no obligation to follow all suggestions,' and addresses the editor's philosophy on preserving voice. _Gap:_ One-shot and unstructured; not persistent, not tunable, not connected to line-level flagging. It is, however, the closest tonal model for Strop's diagnosis cards — name-one-problem-and-ask.

**Strop implications**

- Split the agreement into two first-class halves that do OPPOSITE things: a Continuity Canon (names, traits, timeline, hyphenation decisions) that ENABLES diagnosis cards by letting Strop flag drift ('Ch.1 her eyes are green; Ch.8 they're brown'), and a Voice Charter (leave-alone preferences in the author's own words) that SUPPRESSES cards. One living artifact, two engines — this is the copyeditor's style sheet made executable.
- Make the Voice Charter the headline feature and populate it in the author's own phrasing, mirroring SFWA's 'general note': free-text entries like 'comma splices are my rhythm — never flag them' and 'Dokka's grammar is broken on purpose,' scoped per-character where relevant. This is the anti-homogenization core: the charter is how Strop refuses to average the author to the mean.
- Born empty, zero blocking questions at open (honor 'settings are apologies'): no setup wizard. The opinionated default is to flag everything diagnosable on day one and let the author TEACH Strop what to stop flagging — the agreement accretes from real interactions, exactly as a style sheet grows during a real edit.
- Keep a hard line between 'dismiss this card' (already Strop's never-returns behavior, a per-instance stet) and 'never flag this pattern' (a standing rule the author must explicitly opt into). Never silently promote a dismissal into a rule — instead, after N identical dismissals, surface ONE card: 'You've waved off comma-splice flags 4 times — add to your Voice Charter so I stop?' Inference proposes; the author ratifies.
- Adopt linter anti-abuse governance to prevent the agreement from silencing all criticism: forbid a blanket 'leave my whole manuscript alone' (the 'dots under every line' anti-pattern); every charter rule must name a SPECIFIC pattern; and periodically surface stale or over-broad rules ('You told me never to flag fragments 90 days ago — still right?'), the equivalent of report-unused-disable-directives.
- Treat the CHI-2025 homogenization result as a design red line: because acceptance-driven adaptation is the exact mechanism that erodes voice, Strop must never learn silently from accept/reject to reshape WHAT it suggests. The only thing it may infer is what to STOP flagging, and even that must be confirmed in the open — inference is always visible, reversible, and inspectable, never a black box.
- Host the agreement in the existing version history so it survives restart and forks into a series master: version the charter per book, diff it across the series, and let a named checkpoint snapshot 'the rules as of Draft 3.' This directly answers 'how does it persist across a manuscript and across a series' using infrastructure Strop already has.
- Make every flag explainable on demand (answer the 'crushing my spirit vs. real correction?' anxiety): each diagnosis card should expose 'why I raised this,' and offer a one-tap 'leave this alone' that writes a scoped entry into the charter — turning the moment of friction into the moment the agreement learns, with full transparency.


### tooling-gaps

_The tools writers actually use split into three camps, and none occupies Strop's exact position. (1) Writing environments (Scrivener, Ulysses, iA Writer, Obsidian/Notion, Google Docs, Word) are strong at organizing, drafting and distraction-free flow but offer no craft-level diagnosis; their "history" is either manual per-document snapshots (Scrivener), capped cloud version history (Google Docs allows ~40 named versions), or markup-hell track-changes (Word). (2) AI-native generators (Sudowrite, Novelcrafter, Lex, GrammarlyGO) write FOR you and are documented to flatten voice toward a generic, Western, sycophantic mean — the exact homogenization a 2025 Cornell study quantified and a 2026 MIT Media Lab tool ("Critical Inker") tried to reverse by asking questions instead of generating answers. (3) Diagnostic tools (ProWritingAid, AutoCrit, Fictionary) DO name problems without rewriting — the nearest neighbors to Strop — but they are cloud SaaS (manuscript leaves your machine), they deliver verdicts as score dashboards / 25+ reports benchmarked against published-book corpora (a subtler homogenization-toward-average vector), and they overwhelm rather than ask one question at a time. The white space Strop can own is the precise intersection none of them sit on: local-first / BYO-LLM (text never leaves the machine), diagnosis delivered as Socratic margin cards (one problem + one question, author decides, dismissed-forever), scored against NOTHING so there is no mean to drift toward, plus full keystroke-level history that survives a restart. The craft and HCI literature — editorial-letter practice, "editors highlight, authors correct," iA Writer's own "as ghost writer it takes over and you lose your voice" — independently validates this stance._

**Pain points**

- Generators flatten voice: Sudowrite/Novelcrafter/GrammarlyGO output reads as the generic 'AI voice' and writers are warned to 'accelerate, not replace' their style — meaning the tool's core action is the thing they must undo.
- Diagnostic tools overwhelm: ProWritingAid's 25+ reports cause analysis-paralysis; reviewers' standard advice is to ignore most of them and start with three — a tool fighting its own surface area.
- Score-dashboards homogenize quietly: AutoCrit/Fictionary benchmark prose against published-book corpora and 38-element rubrics, nudging idiosyncratic writers toward the median of what's already been published.
- Rule-checkers punish craft: fiction writers burn editing time clicking 'Ignore' on Grammarly flags for intentional fragments, comma splices, second-person, and italic interiority.
- Sycophancy makes AI feedback worthless: writers report AI 'just tells them their work is wonderful', so they must prompt-engineer brutality to get any real critique.
- Privacy dread: unpublished/memoir/trauma writers fear (with documented cause) that uploaded drafts become training data; opt-out toggles are distrusted after real leaks.
- Fragile/destructive history: Scrivener snapshots are manual and per-document with an un-undoable rollback; Word track-changes is 'markup hell' that can corrupt documents; Google Docs caps named versions.
- Setup tax: Obsidian/Notion writers build vaults for weeks and never write the scene; every basic feature is a plugin with conflict risk.
- Collaboration/editor-handoff is bolted on: Scrivener has no live co-editing; receiving a track-changes file is described as immediately 'overwhelming'.
- No tool delivers feedback the way a human developmental editor does — one named problem plus a question, in the margin, that the author can dismiss for good.

**Tool landscape**

- **Scrivener** — Binder + corkboard + manual Snapshots + Compile; the canonical long-form organizer bought once, run locally. _Gap:_ Snapshots are manual, per-active-document, and rollback is irreversible; no continuous keystroke history; no craft diagnosis; no live collaboration; Dropbox-only sync; dated UI with a steep learning curve.
- **Ulysses** — Markdown library + goals + clean export; subscription, Apple-only, distraction-free flow. _Gap:_ No diagnosis, no voice feedback, no real version history; locked to Apple + subscription; purely an environment, not an editor.
- **iA Writer** — Minimal Markdown editor with local Authorship color-coding of human/AI/source text; explicit anti-ghostwriter philosophy. _Gap:_ Closest philosophical ally but offers ZERO craft feedback — it labels who wrote what; it never names a problem or asks a question.
- **Google Docs** — Cloud collaboration with Suggesting mode, comments, and automatic version history. _Gap:_ Cloud-only (privacy non-starter for sensitive drafts); named versions capped (~40); comments/suggestions pile into clutter; no craft-level diagnosis; suggestions are human-sourced edits, not voice-preserving questions.
- **Microsoft Word** — Track Changes + comments; the publishing-industry handoff standard for line/copyedits. _Gap:_ 'Markup hell' — overwhelming, can corrupt documents around tables/footnotes; accept/reject is binary not Socratic; no diagnosis; history is the edit-markup itself, not a timeline.
- **Notion / Obsidian** — Blank-slate local (Obsidian) or cloud (Notion) knowledge bases adapted for writing via plugins/templates. _Gap:_ High setup overhead; core writing features each need a plugin; 'never write a scene' failure mode; no opinionated defaults; no diagnosis; opposite of Strop's zero-friction-at-open principle.
- **Sudowrite** — AI-first fiction generator — Story Bible, Write/Describe/Rewrite, bundled models. _Gap:_ Generates text (Strop's anti-feature); documented voice homogenization and 'AI voice'; cloud; treats chapters in isolation so voice drifts; output needs heavy editing to de-genericize.
- **Novelcrafter** — Writing-software-first with Codex wiki + BYO-LLM generation and chat over scenes. _Gap:_ BYO-LLM is right, but it is built around GENERATING prose and managing AI context (Voice Profiles) rather than diagnosing the author's own draft; cloud app; still text-generation at its center.
- **ProWritingAid** — 25+ analytic reports (style, overused words, sentence length, pacing) plus AI rewrite/critique. _Gap:_ Report overload causes analysis-paralysis; rule-based flags fight craft choices and 'flatten your voice'; cloud; benchmarks toward an average; verdicts as metrics, not questions.
- **Grammarly / GrammarlyGO** — Real-time grammar/style correction that pivoted into generation, rewrite, and 'Expert Review' author-simulation. _Gap:_ Optimizes for corporate clarity, flattening narrative voice; fiction writers click 'Ignore' constantly; pivoted toward generation and consent-violating author impersonation — moving away from honest diagnosis.
- **Lex** — Clean editor with ++ AI continuations tuned to your style and on-demand critique; thinking-partner positioning. _Gap:_ Still completes/generates text; cloud; no version history, no suggestion-tracking; critique is chat-driven not a persistent dismissable margin of named problems.
- **AutoCrit** — Diagnostic story analyzer that tells you what's wrong without rewriting; explicitly anti auto-rewrite. _Gap:_ Nearest neighbor on stance, but cloud SaaS; benchmarks your prose against 'patterns across published books' (homogenizes toward published mean); reports/scores, not one-problem-one-question Socratic cards; fiction-rubric-bound.

**Strop implications**

- Own the exact intersection, not diagnosis alone: AutoCrit/Fictionary already 'diagnose, don't rewrite', so Strop must lead with the COMBINATION they can't match — local-first/BYO-Ollama + Socratic margin cards + score-against-nothing + persistent keystroke history. Pitch it as 'the developmental editor that runs on your machine and never benchmarks you against the average.'
- Make 'we score you against nothing' a headline anti-feature. Every diagnostic rival compares you to a published-book corpus or a 38-element rubric — a homogenization vector by design. Strop naming a problem WITHOUT a metric/percentile is the differentiator the Cornell finding demands.
- Diagnosis card = one named problem + one question, never a rule verdict. Encode 'Editors highlight; authors correct.' Ban score badges, grades, and 'X% of bestsellers do Y' framing — those are exactly what overwhelms ProWritingAid users and flattens voice.
- Lean hard on local-first as a trust moat for the most underserved, highest-stakes writers: memoir, trauma, unpublished novels. 'Your manuscript never leaves the machine on the local path' answers a documented, specific fear no cloud rival can credibly match.
- Ship iA Writer's philosophy as ACTION. iA proved the market shares Strop's 'lose your voice' worldview but only color-codes authorship. Strop is the missing second half: the same philosophy that actually engages the craft.
- Persistent, keystroke-level, restart-surviving history with named checkpoints is a clean win — every incumbent's history is manual (Scrivener), capped (Google Docs), or destructive/markup-hell (Word). Market it as safety for an ADHD-minded writer who fears losing work, reinforcing 'enough support, not neutrality.'
- Dismissed-forever cards are the antidote to ProWritingAid's 25-report overwhelm and Word's accept/reject fatigue. Make 'a dismissed card never returns' a visible promise — the tool gets quieter as you work, the opposite of analytic dashboards.
- Refuse generation in v1 and say why out loud. The market is stampeding toward generators and author-impersonation (Grammarly Expert Review backlash); diagnosis-only is a contrarian, trust-building position. 'Strop will never write the sentence for you' is a feature, not a limitation.
- Borrow 'productive friction' framing from MIT's Critical Inker explicitly: questions over answers preserve the author's thinking and prevent cognitive deskilling. This is academic cover for the whole product thesis.
- Avoid the blank-slate trap (Obsidian/Notion): zero blocking questions at open, opinionated defaults, no plugin-vault setup ritual. The competitor failure mode is 'three weeks configuring, never wrote a scene' — Strop should land the writer in the document instantly.
