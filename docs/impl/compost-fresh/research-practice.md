# Digest — how writers actually handle cut material and stray ideas

Topic: the human practice, independent of software. Web-verified 2026-07-05; every claim cited.

## 1 · The practices, inventoried

**The bottom-of-document dump.** The brief's null baseline is not a strawman; it is the single most common real practice. Of 15 working writers surveyed by [Reproof](https://www.reproof.app/blog/writing-and-the-cutting-room-floor), all did one of three things: move cuts to the end of the document ("a piggy bank at the bottom of anything I write" — Nate Martins), start a new document with the best cuts, or delete without mercy. A [viral lawyer thread](https://convopage.com/c/1307023357150453760) shows the same three camps outside fiction, plus a lifecycle: scraps start "at the end of the document under the heading 'cut(s)'", then at review-time move to a doc called "*[original file]* cuts".

**The cuts file.** Steven Pressfield keeps a per-manuscript [CULLS file](https://stevenpressfield.com/2015/06/files-i-work-with-5/): "everything I've cut… stashed in literary purgatory." Two findings are load-bearing: "**The file is always longer than the book**" (194-page manuscript, 263-page CULLS), and "**I'll almost never bring something back… out of 250 pages, maybe thirty or forty lines.**" Retrieval is memory-triggered, not browse-triggered: "I'll be shaving… and a voice will speak up: 'Remember the part where the dog bites the football?'" — then Command-F finds it. Novelists echo the shape under affectionate names: Matt Gemmell's per-project "Deleted Scenes" folder ("they stay there forever"), Therese Walsh's [Golden Dumpster](https://writerunboxed.com/2007/10/16/the-golden-dumpster/) (a curated tier above her "Dead Files"; "generally my old scenes don't make it back"), a poet's ["Shit File"](https://nickesmith.com/in-praise-of-the-shit-file/), forum users' ["Boneyard — gone but not forgotten"](https://www.writingforums.com/threads/the-scrap-file.163934/), and *The Good Place* writers' room "[Candy Bag](https://www.reproof.app/blog/writing-and-the-cutting-room-floor)" — deliberately gentler than "cutting-room floor."

**"Kill your darlings"** is Arthur Quiller-Couch, 1914 ("Murder your darlings" — [Slate](https://slate.com/culture/2013/10/kill-your-darlings-writing-advice-what-writer-really-said-to-murder-your-babies.html); routinely misattributed to Faulkner). The cuts file is the folk mitigation of that advice (§2).

**Screenwriters' boneyard.** Scenes cut from the shooting script, "stored in the order they were cut, ready to be resurrected" ([StudioBinder](https://www.studiobinder.com/blog/what-is-the-boneyard-stripboard/)). Crucially, the plain-text [Fountain format](https://fountain.io/syntax/) *formalized* it: `/* … */` wraps any span — whole scenes — "ignored completely on formatted output"; the boneyard is the one syntax allowed to span the entire file. Fountain has a *separate* syntax for notes-to-self: `[[…]]`, inline, also excluded from output. One writer-designed format, two distinct tenses, both living inside the one document.

**Journalists' asides live inline**: the [TK convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/To_come_(publishing)) ("to come") parks a missing fact *in place* with a letter-pair nearly absent from English; one search at the end finds every hole. The aside practice is a searchable inline marker, not a pile.

**Journalists' morgue files** are a different animal: an *organized reference archive* — subject-labelled clipping envelopes, professionally maintained (the Herald Tribune's held [15 million](https://briscoecenter.org/research/online-reference-tools/new-york-media-morgues/)) — research memory, not parked prose. It died when its maintainers (librarians) were cut ([CJR](https://www.cjr.org/tow_center_reports/the-dire-state-of-news-archiving-in-the-digital-age.php)).

**Seed-keeping** is its own lineage: [commonplace books](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonplace_book) (Locke's 1685 method; global, topic-indexed); [Lamott's index cards](http://www.43folders.com/2005/04/04/anne-lamott-on-index-cards) (one folded in the back pocket, everywhere; "when stumped I review them and they jog my memory"); Steven Johnson's [Spark File](https://medium.com/the-writers-room/the-spark-file-8d6e7df7ae58) — one global chronological document of hunches, **no organizing**, re-read whole every 3–4 months, which is where the 2008 idea meets its 2012 missing piece; Luhmann's [zettelkasten](https://zettelkasten.de/introduction/) (~90,000 processed, linked notes — the expert extreme, not the writer norm); Twyla Tharp's [per-project box](https://fortelabs.com/blog/the-box-twyla-tharp-on-project-based-organizing/) ("The box means I never have to worry about forgetting… I know where to find it"). [Nabokov's index cards](https://www.theparisreview.org/interviews/4310/the-art-of-fiction-no-40-vladimir-nabokov) were not scraps — they were the manuscript itself, composed non-linearly.

**Goldberg's "composting"** ([Writing Down the Bones](https://writeordiemag.com/features/10-writing-lessons-learned-from-writing-down-the-bones-freeing-the-writer-within-by-natalie-goldberg)) is about *time*, not storage: experience "sifts through consciousness" and decomposes into fertile soil; she filled notebooks for months about her father dying before "a long poem poured out." Retrieval = re-reading whole notebooks and marking live bits, not lookup. [Didion's notebook](https://www.themarginalian.org/2012/11/19/joan-didion-on-keeping-a-notebook/) adds the identity function: "keeping in touch is what notebooks are all about" — value without any retrieval at all.

## 2 · Q1: permission or archive?

**Permission, overwhelmingly — but it only works because retrieval is genuinely possible.** "Removing words to a separate file… eases the pain of separation and makes killing those darlings just a little bit easier" ([Helping Writers Become Authors](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/writers-shouldnt-delete/)); "I find it easier to move on if I can retrieve what I wrote, **even if I never do**" ([Writing Forums](https://www.writingforums.com/threads/the-scrap-file.163934/)); "It's easier to cut things when you know they're not disappearing forever" ([Write by the Sea](https://www.writebythesea.com/your-words-deserve-better-than-the-delete-key-set-up-a-writers-scraps-file)); boneyards let screenwriters "cut liberally… secure in the knowledge that no idea is ever truly lost."

**Measured retrieval:** Pressfield: 30–40 lines out of 250 pages (~1–2%). A [published-author roundup](http://www.wastepaperprose.com/2012/10/author-insight-story-scraps.html): "So far I haven't gone back" (Appelhans); "most of the things in that folder never get used again" (Doller); vs "often I do use them later — if not the whole scene, a snippet, or even just a turn of phrase" (Peloquin). **When retrieval happens it is phrase- or sentence-grained, memory-triggered, found by search — almost never a whole scene restored to its old slot.** The documented afterlife of whole scenes is elsewhere: another project's genesis, or reader-facing extras (Pullman's "Lantern Slides").

## 3 · Q2: one pile or three tenses?

Heavy practitioners **do** separate — but not into three piles. The real fault line is **inline vs pile vs upstream**: *asides* stay inline at the point they concern (TK; Fountain `[[notes]]`); *cuts* go to a pile ordered by cut-time, one per manuscript; *seeds* live upstream in a global notebook/spark file that predates any manuscript. Casual writers keep one bucket and it works ("I just call mine Notes"). Two-tier curation appears spontaneously (Dead Files → Golden Dumpster; scraps kept "with the date and a quick note: *cut from chapter 2 — too much backstory*").

## 4 · Q3: per-project or global?

Clean gradient: **the closer to a manuscript, the more it lives with that manuscript.** Cuts: per-project, universally (CULLS, Deleted Scenes folder, "*[file]* cuts"; the Fountain boneyard is literally in the file). Asides: in the draft itself. Seeds: global (spark file, commonplace book, cards) — except Tharp's box, which shows seeds go per-project once a project is committed. Per-document matches cuts and asides perfectly; a global seed pile is the one tense it can't serve.

## 5 · Q4: what makes it stick vs die?

**Sticks:** (a) capture at the point of writing with zero filing decision — bottom-of-doc, TK, the card in the back pocket; (b) one container, no taxonomy — Johnson is explicit that the *absence* of organization is the feature, and one chronological file "forces you to scroll through the whole list" on every add; (c) a re-reading ritual attached to an existing workflow moment — the lawyer who "always reads back over the scrap at the end of drafting to make sure nothing should go back in," Johnson's quarterly re-read, Nick Smith's "periodic review"; (d) a name that changes how cutting feels (Candy Bag). **Dies:** any system demanding processing or organization at capture — the [collector's fallacy](https://zettelkasten.de/posts/collectors-fallacy/) ("piling up copies until the stack becomes unmanageable, after which it will be ignored entirely"), the second-brain [note graveyards](https://anshulkumar.substack.com/p/your-notes-are-a-graveyard-heres) ("elaborate funnels with no drain"), the fragment database one writer calls "the most time-consuming, writing-avoidance-behaviour way imaginable" ([Writers' Greenhouse](https://blog.thewritersgreenhouse.co.uk/2014/05/things-ive-learnt-keep-your-fragments.html): "storing fragments isn't the same as being able to find them"); and losing the maintainer (the morgues died with the librarians).

## 6 · Ranked design implications (F1–F8)

1. **F1 is the whole game.** The practice is born at the moment a cut hurts; the file's chief product is *permission*, consumed at cut-time. Spend the design budget on a one-gesture, caret-stays park with visible compliance — not on retrieval UI (§2).
2. **F8 = F1 done right, plus zero filing.** Any capture-time decision (category? tag? title?) kills the habit (§5). The invitation is the park verb resting on the selection menu when a writer first hesitates over a deletion — the null baseline's own gesture, made cheaper.
3. **F4/F3: build for search-and-skim, not browse.** Retrieval is ~1–2%, phrase-grained, memory-triggered, executed by find (§2). Automatic cut-time provenance (when, from-where — never asked for) is what makes a skim legible months later. Weight F4 as the cheap inverse P13 demands, no more.
4. **F7: split inline-vs-pile, not three piles.** Asides want to stay *at* the text they concern (TK, `[[notes]]`); parkings want a cut-ordered pile; global seeds are the tense per-document Strop can't honestly serve — fold seeds into jots (Tharp mode: seeds of *this* piece) or cut them explicitly (§3, §4).
5. **F5: Fountain proves the in-document region works** — writers already accept non-manuscript text inside the one file *when output-scoping is automatic*. Formalizing the boundary the writer already draws (counts, export, AI, cold read excluding it visibly) is exactly where a design beats the null baseline (§1).
6. **F6: name and temperature carry the split.** Practice distinguishes the *living* pile (Candy Bag, warm, editable) from the *dead record* (morgue, boneyard). Writers already think in this two-afterlife grammar; scrapyard = warm and writer-owned, graveyard = drained and automatic (§1).
7. **F3: the one reliable retrieval moment is the end-of-revision sweep** ("read the scrap before calling it done"). A pile that rests adjacent and skimmable — without a prompt (P2) — serves the ritual that keeps files alive (§5).
8. **F2: pocket-card economics.** Lamott's card is captured without leaving the sidewalk; a jot must cost less than the thought, caret never leaving (§1).
