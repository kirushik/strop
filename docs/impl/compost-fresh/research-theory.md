# Research digest — HCI & design theory for an out-of-manuscript scrapyard

Web-verified 2026-07-05. Companion to `brief.md`. Every claim carries its source.

## 1 · Capture is a self-interruption — the science of the jot (F1, F2)

The definitive field study of exactly our text is [Bernstein, Van Kleek, Karger & schraefel, "Information Scraps" (ACM TOIS 26:4, 2008)](https://hci.stanford.edu/publications/2008/infoscraps/TOIS-final-with-refs.pdf). Across 27 knowledge workers, ideas, notes and reminders ended up on post-its, self-emails and stray text files — *outside* purpose-built tools — because the winning media offer **ease of capture, flexibility (no schema imposed), and availability at the moment of need**. Tools lose the instant capture demands a filing decision, category, or destination. Strongest single warrant for F2: the jot must accept raw text with zero classification, or writers keep the divider-at-the-bottom.

Cognitive-science backing: in the memory-for-goals model, a suspended goal decays and is re-retrieved via **cues**; resumption after brief, low-demand interruptions costs seconds, not minutes, when a cue is present ([Altmann & Trafton, CogSci 2004](https://www.interruptions.net/literature/Altmann-CogSci04.pdf)). Cost grows with interruption **duration and demand** ([Monk, Trafton & Boehm-Davis 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19102614/)); even an ~8-second preparation lag before diverting speeds resumption ([Trafton et al. 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23261418/)). Translation: the cheapest jot is one where **the screen never changes** — the untouched caret, selection and scroll position *are* the resumption cue; capture that navigates away destroys the cue and buys the full switch cost. Beware the folkloric "23 minutes to refocus": [Mark, Gudith & Klocke (CHI 2008)](https://ics.uci.edu/~gmark/chi08-mark.pdf) measured time to return to an interrupted *sphere of work* amid other whole tasks — people compensate by working faster, at the price of stress — not a per-jot cost ([debunk](https://blog.oberien.de/2023/11/05/23-minutes-15-seconds.html)). Roughly 44% of interruptions are self-initiated; the jot will happen — the tool's job is to make it cost seconds.

[Raskin's *The Humane Interface*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Humane_Interface) supplies the gesture grammar. An interface is **modal** when system state is not the user's locus of attention yet changes what a gesture does; mode errors are inevitable because habituation is inevitable and desirable — habits free attention for the text. His remedy is the **quasimode**: state held only by continuous user action (Shift is the canonical case), which cannot be forgotten ([Mode (user interface), Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mode_(user_interface))). Implications: the capture gesture must be *monotonous* — exactly one way, identical everywhere (P8); a hold-to-peek at the scrapyard is a lawful quasimode (P9-compliant: it only enlarges the visible); habituated gestures, above all Esc, are never repurposed.

## 2 · One document, many views — Engelbart against the drawer (F5, F6)

The brief calls the null baseline "very Engelbart," and it is exactly half of him. In NLS/Augment every file was one structured document, but nobody saw it raw: **viewspecs** — per-window view specifications — clipped it live by level, truncated statements to one line, filtered content ([Doug Engelbart Institute, "Viewspecs"](https://www.dougengelbart.org/content/view/218/158/); [1984 Augment authorship paper](https://www.dougengelbart.org/pubs/papers/scanned-original/1984-oad-2250-Authorship-Support-Provisions-in-Augment.pdf)). The baseline's missing half is thus not a new container but **machine-known structure plus view control**: once the tool knows where "manuscript" ends, cold read, export, counts and AI scope become viewspecs over one file — beating baseline failures 1, 2 and 6 while keeping its virtues (one file, always saved, searchable, everything text per P3). The graveyard's receded one-liners are already truncation-viewspecs; a scrapyard can speak the same dialect (F6).

[Ted Nelson](https://xanadu.com.au/ted/XU/XuPageKeio.html) sharpens what moved text should remember: in Xanadu a link connects things that are *different*; a **transclusion** connects things that are *the same* — the moved or quoted portion keeps a path back to its origin and context ([Project Xanadu, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Xanadu)); **parallel documents** show visible connections side by side ([Xanalogical structure](https://mprove.de/visionreality/media/xuDation.html)). Translation: a parked passage is the same text relocated, not a copy in a void; retaining origin makes F4 retrieval and P13's inverse structural, exactly as the graveyard's "Put back" behaves.

[Bush's memex (1945)](https://www.w3.org/History/1945/vbush/vbush7.shtml) adds the retrieval theory: an "enlarged intimate supplement to memory" whose essential feature is **associative trails**, not classification — items are re-found by association at the moment of need. With the brief's evidence that retrieval is rare, this says: invest in *re-reading by association* (skim, adjacency, provenance), not taxonomy or heavy search.

## 3 · Piles — informal accumulation is a first-class organization (F3, F7)

[Malone's 1983 desk study](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/220515815_Malone_T_How_Do_People_Organize_Their_Desks_Implications_for_the_Design_of_Office_Information_Systems_ACM_Transactions_on_Office_Information_Systems_11_99-112) split office organization into **files** (titled, ordered, for finding) and **piles** (untitled, spatial, for *reminding* and deferred classification — a short-term memory you can see). Apple's Human Interface Group built the canonical digital pile: [Mander, Salomon & Wong, CHI 1992](http://www.cs.columbia.edu/~feiner/courses/csw4170/resources/p627-mander.pdf) — users piled precisely the material they were *not ready to name*, browsed by edge-riffling; "piling requires less mental effort" than filing, and piles complement, not replace, the formal archive. [Marshall & Shipman's VIKI (ECHT 1994)](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/VIKI:-spatial-hypertext-supporting-emergent-Marshall-Shipman/76dba0d2a151e248c33c54a94b64d7157932df0e) and their ["Formality Considered Harmful" (1993)](https://people.engr.tamu.edu/shipman/viki/papers/vkat-papers.html) generalize: users resist premature formalization; ambiguous, *emerging* structure is best expressed implicitly — position, adjacency, grouping — and formalized late or never ([Spatial hypertext: designing for change, CACM 1995](https://dl.acm.org/doi/abs/10.1145/208344.208350)).

Bearing on F7: seeds/parkings/asides is a taxonomy the *brief* can see but the writer at capture time often cannot ("no longer dead-sure gone" is by definition unclassifiable). The literature says: one pile, recency-ordered, classification deferred — and since everything is text (P3), blank lines and ordering give emergent structure free. The cautionary tale is [BumpTop (Agarawala & Balakrishnan, CHI 2006)](https://www.dgp.toronto.edu/papers/aagarawala_CHI2006.pdf): charming full-physics piles, [acquired by Google in 2010 and discontinued](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BumpTop); the pile *semantics* (casual, unnamed, riffle-able) survived everywhere, the physical *chrome* did not. Metaphor fidelity is not utility — every ounce of container rendering must beat the baseline somewhere nameable (brief §3).

## 4 · Periphery, glanceability, and the cost of hiding (F5, F8)

[Matthews et al. on glanceable peripheral displays](https://digitalassets.lib.berkeley.edu/techreports/ucb/text/EECS-2006-113.pdf) ([DIS 2006](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/1142405.1142457)): a periphery works when information is **abstracted** (one-liners, counts) so a glance suffices and the primary task keeps focus. A scrapyard at rest should read at a glance and never steal it — the margin's recede behaviour is already this pattern.

But periphery must not mean *hidden*. [NN/g's 179-participant study](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/hamburger-menus/) found hiding navigation **cuts discoverability roughly in half**; on desktop, hidden menus were used in 27% of cases versus 48–50% for visible ones. For F8 this is decisive: a fully concealed edge drawer cannot invite a writer who never kept scraps. The invitation must be a **visible resting edge** — a seam, a region boundary, a footer chip like the graveyard's — discovered by eye, not tour (P2, P5). [Progressive disclosure (Nielsen)](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/progressive-disclosure/) supplies the depth gradient: showing only the container's face improves learnability and error rate (Carroll's IBM "training wheels," 1983–84: hiding advanced function early improved later success); counts, provenance, restore reveal one notch at a time.

## 5 · What writing cognition says the scrapyard is *for* (F2, F7, F8)

[Flower & Hayes, "A Cognitive Process Theory of Writing" (CCC 32:4, 1981)](https://www.scirp.org/reference/referencespapers?referenceid=1541447): composing is not linear stages but **planning, translating and reviewing recursively embedded** under a monitor, the writer juggling constraints against a growing goal network. Ideas surface *during* translation that belong to planning — they don't fit the sentence under the hand; unbuffered, they are lost or force a switch. The scrapyard is that buffer externalized: it serves *generating*, so it must be reachable from drafting without entering anything resembling review. And writers already own a native container metaphor — not the drawer or shelf (office furniture) but the **facing page**: [Ann Berthoff's double-entry / dialectical notebook](http://www.comppile.org/archives/fforum/fforum3(2)files/fforum3(2)Berthoff.pdf), notes on one side, thinking-about-notes opposite, a "continuing audit of meaning" ([Writing@CSU](https://writing.colostate.edu/guides/teaching/e238/doubleex.cfm)). A parallel text surface beside the manuscript has centuries of writerly practice behind it; drawers and piles have offices.

## Ranked design implications

1. **Schema-free, in-place capture** (Bernstein; Altmann & Trafton) — F2/F1: one gesture, no destination or category decision, caret and scroll untouched; the unchanged screen is the resumption cue. The baseline is weakest here (failures 3–4); the feature is won or lost here.
2. **Boundary + views, not a new container** (Engelbart viewspecs) — F5: make the tool *know* the manuscript's edge inside the one file, then scope export/counts/AI/cold-read as views. Beats baseline failures 1, 2, 6 with near-zero new concepts and keeps P3 intact.
3. **Moved text keeps a path home** (Nelson transclusion; P13) — F1/F4/F6: a parked passage is the same text relocated, origin remembered; retrieval and "put back" share one grammar with the graveyard.
4. **One pile, classification deferred** (Malone; Mander et al.; Marshall & Shipman) — F7: a single recency-ordered home; the seed/parking/aside distinction stays emergent (position, writer's own words), never a forced picker at capture.
5. **Visible edge, quiet depth** (NN/g; Nielsen; Matthews) — F8/F5: the container's edge rests in sight (hiding halves discovery — a closed drawer cannot invite); contents stay glanceable one-liners; depth arrives by progressive disclosure, never dialogue (P2, P5).
6. **Skim is the retrieval engine** (Bush; Mander's riffle) — F3: cheap riffle/expand of one-liners with guaranteed Esc-home return; association and adjacency before search or tags.
7. **Protect habituation; prefer quasimodes** (Raskin) — all flows: one verb one gesture everywhere (P8), Esc sacred, peeking may be spring-loaded; never a mode whose state isn't the locus of attention (P6, P12).
8. **Spend no chrome on metaphor fidelity** (BumpTop) — all flows: pile semantics without pile theatre; any rendering that merely *matches* the divider-at-the-bottom loses to it (brief §3).
