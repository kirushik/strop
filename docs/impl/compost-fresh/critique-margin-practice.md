# Critique — design-margin.md ("Set aside"), practice panel

Two voices. Verdict at the end.

## The Novelist

I keep a cuts file. I know why I keep it, and this design has the *why*
backwards.

**The wound stays visible — and that inverts the feature's whole
function.** The research is unambiguous: parking's product is
permission, consumed at cut-time, and permission works because the text
*leaves* — "eases the pain of separation," "literary purgatory," a
piggy bank at the *bottom*. Every real practice moves the darling out
of the working view. This design pins its first words, warm and glowing,
beside the exact paragraph I'm now rewriting without it. That is not
purgatory; that is a memorial at the amputation site. The design names
this loss (§6) and offers the one-line recede as anaesthetic, but the
one line *is the darling's opening phrase* — the most memorable part,
the part that made it a darling. Test: park a passage, then rewrite the
paragraph beside the seam; the cut argues with you the whole time. Some
of us will delete instead of park just to make it stop — recreating
baseline failure 3, the very failure F1 exists to fix.

**Month three: the hoard breaks the geometry, not just the aesthetics.**
Pressfield's CULLS is longer than the book, and cuts don't distribute —
revision is local. The chapter I'm gutting is exactly where ten
set-asides land, in a lane that is already rationed (notes, machine
cards, an over-budget recede rule). The design says clusters "displace
down-lane" — so the card for the cut on page 40 sits beside page 42.
The *entire bet* was "the scrap rests beside the seam it left"; under
the load the feature is designed to invite, that claim is false. Testable:
ten parks from one viewport at 800 pt with three notes and two AI cards
present — count how many one-liners still touch their seam.

**The sweep ritual dies — and the dead read better than the living.**
The one retrieval moment that keeps a cuts file alive is the
end-of-revision read-through ("I always read back over the scrap at the
end of drafting"). Here that is a full-document margin patrol, hunting
warm lines interleaved with two other card kinds. Meanwhile the
graveyard — the *dead* record — offers exactly the contiguous tail read
my living scraps are denied. The tool gathers what I discarded by
accident and scatters what I saved on purpose. That asymmetry is
perverse, and §6's "in-context sweep is better" is the designer talking,
not a writer: "should this go back in?" during a sweep is a question
about the *piece as it now stands*, answered by reading the pile against
the whole, not by re-encountering each stump in situ.

**Seeds are mis-anchored.** A stray thought mid-sentence struck *then*,
not *there*. Anchoring it to the sentence under the caret is time
masquerading as relevance; my margin fills with notes that concern
nothing near them. The spark file's power is one chronological list
re-read whole; the design concedes this, then scores F7 "matches." It
doesn't. The baseline's bottom section is a strictly better seed home.
F7 loses.

What survives my read: F1's economy is real (one chord, no travel), the
jot's unchanged-screen return is right, and find reaching into scraps —
"12 in the piece · 3 set aside" — is exactly how Pressfield actually
retrieves. Keep those.

## The Spartan

My vote walks in as SHELVE. This design earns more respect than most,
and I'll say why before I cut it.

**The rent-payer is the boundary, not the cards.** The one honest idea
here: the column/margin boundary is already known to every scope —
count, export, reviewer, cold read exclude the margin *today*.
Baseline failures 1, 2, 5, 6 evaporate structurally, no checkbox, no
new region to teach four subsystems. That is a genuine beat, achieved
by *reusing* structure — the least chrome of any conceivable answer.
Whatever ships, this insight should be in it.

Now the cuts.

**Storage in a rationed lane.** The margin has a budget; the product's
own rules recede older cards when it's exceeded. This design moves
*storage* — unbounded, permanent, longer-than-the-book storage — into a
lane whose defining property is scarcity, then assigns it lowest
priority. Lowest priority in a saturated lane means first evicted from
view, and an invisible resting place is the Spike: the design cites
that failure mode itself, then builds its shelf in the one place the
product is contractually allowed to push things out of. The null
baseline's bottom pile never evicts anything.

**Two warm one-liners, one typeface apart.** At receded scale, a note
and a set-aside are the same colour, same lane, same one-line form; the
distinction is note-typography versus manuscript-typography plus a word
count, truncated at 800 pt. P10 demands form corroborate colour;
"commentary about the story" versus "piece of the story standing
outside it" is an ontological split carried by a font choice a stranger
won't register. Screenshot test at 800 pt: show both receded; if the
stranger can't sort them, the scene in §2 is wishful.

**The scorecard is graded generously.** F3 "beats" holds only for
scraps near the hand; the deliberate skim of *everything* loses to the
baseline's contiguous pile. F7 is a loss dressed as a match (seeds).
F6 "beats" claims one spatial story, but sideways-scattered living
versus tail-gathered dead is two stories. Honest ledger: F1, F2, F5
beat; F4 beats narrowly (put-back-at-origin); F3 splits; F6 half;
F7 loses. That is still a positive ledger — which is why my vote,
grudgingly, is not shelve. The boundary reuse plus a one-chord park
beats zero UI on the failures the brief itself enumerates. But the
card apparatus must shrink to fit the lane it borrows, or the lane
will make my argument for me.

## Panel verdict

**Needs surgery; beats the baseline** — on scope purity and park
economy — while losing the pile, the forgetting, and the seeds.

**Kill-shots**
1. Heavy-parker geometry collapse: ten parks from one viewport +
   existing cards → one-liners displaced off their seams; the core
   "beside the seam it left" claim fails under the load the feature
   invites. (Test as stated.)
2. Inverted emotional function: parked darlings stay in peripheral view
   at the rewrite site; permission-to-forget becomes enforced
   remembering; predictable delete-instead-of-park regression.
3. The sweep ritual has no home: no gathered reading of the living pile
   exists, while the dead graveyard reads contiguously — retrieval's one
   reliable ritual is served worse than the baseline.
4. Receded set-aside vs receded note indistinguishable at 800 pt
   (typeface + count only) — fails P10's form-corroboration and the §2
   screenshot claim.
5. Seeds anchored by time-of-capture masquerading as place-of-relevance;
   F7 scored "matches" is actually a loss.

**Grafts**
1. *The gathered lens*: a view, not a place — an omnibar/cold-read-style
   pass that reads every set-aside as one pile (the clickable "3 set
   aside" count is the door). Serves the sweep without new surface.
2. *Sink to the tail*: over-budget or writer-flicked set-asides drift to
   a warm, editable living section adjacent to the graveyard — living
   pile above dead record; hoard capacity unbounded; forgetting served.
3. *Keep the boundary-reuse core*: scope exclusion inherited from the
   column/margin split must survive into any winning design.
4. *Find-first put-back*: a find match inside a set-aside offers Put
   back from the result — Pressfield's Command-F retrieval made
   first-class.
5. *Cluster chip*: multiple parks from one region collapse into one
   stacked "3 set aside · 512 words" line, protecting the lane budget
   and the seam claim.
